Facts
  • Tarsometatarsal articulation is known as the Lisfranc joint
  • 20% of these injuries are missed in polytrauma patients
  • Most commonly due to axial loading of a plantarflexed foot

  • Lisfranc joint represents border between midfoot and forefoot
  • Undergoes only slight motion: 10°-20°
    • 4th and 5th metatarsocuboid joints have more movement than the 1st, 2nd and 3rd tarsometatarsal joints
    • This motion is important for normal foot mechanics
  • Base of the 2nd MT is wider dorsally and recessed between medial and lateral cuneiform
    • Confers inherent stability
  • Trapezoidal cross-section of the middle three MTs prevents plantar displacement and forms a transverse arch for which the 2nd MT is the keystone
  • Strong ligamentous connections between MTs and tarsal bones
    • Plantar ligaments are stronger
    • 2nd-5th MTs are connected by intermetatarsal ligaments
    • 1st and 2nd MTs are not connected
    • Most important is the Lisfranc ligament which attaches the plantar aspect of the medial cuneiform to the base of the 2nd MT
  • Two column concept: structural description of the foot
    • Medial: 1st, 2nd and 3rd MTs, the 3 cuneiforms, navicular and talus
    • Lateral: 4th and 5th MTs, cuboid and calcaneus
  • Foot compartments:
    • Medial: abductor hallucis, flexor hallucis brevis
    • Interosseous: 7 interossei muscles
    • Central superficial: flexor digitorum brevis, adductor hallucis, lumbricals
    • Central deep (calcaneal): quadratus plantae
    • Lateral: flexor digiti minimi brevis, abductor digiti minimi, opponens digiti minimi
  • Dorsalis pedis artery dives between the 1st and 2nd MT bases and is at risk for injury
  • Injuries often due to twisting, crush or axial loading

  • Patients complain of midfoot pain and demonstrate swelling and tenderness to palpation over the midfoot/forefoot junction
  • High-energy mechanisms warrant a full trauma evaluation and resuscitation
  • Spontaneous reduction prior to presentation may occur
  • Plantar ecchymosis suggests ligamentous injury
  • Passive ROM of the digits may elicit pain at the tarsometatarsal joint
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
    • Up to 95% have MT fractures
    • Up to 40% have tarsal fractures
  • Compartment syndrome: sustained increase in compartment pressure due to inflammation and edema in one or more of the foot compartments leading to vascular compromise and muscle death
    • Crush injuries are particularly susceptible
    • Signs include diminished pulses, severe swelling and pain out of proportion to injury, particularly with passive extension of the digits
    • Compartment pressures should be measured when signs of compartment syndrome are present
    • Urgent fasciotomy of the compartments is warranted for pressures within 30 mmHg of the diastolic blood pressure
