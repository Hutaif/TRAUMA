Treatment
  • Urgent concentric reduction is required to reduce risk of osteonecrosis of the femoral head and compression of the sciatic nerve
    • Risk of osteonecrosis increases in hips unreduced for > 6 hours
  • Delay > 12 hours worsens long-term prognosis significantly
  • Associated femoral head and acetabular fractures can be treated subacutely
  • Do not attempt closed reduction in the presence of ipsilateral femoral neck fractures
    • Achieve provisional fixation first in the operating room then gentle reduction followed by definitive fixation of the femoral neck

  • Closed reduction:
    • Performed with patient under either general anesthesia or conscious sedation
      • Sedation must achieve adequate muscle relaxation and pain control to minimize risk of further injury by the reduction maneuver
    • Gentle manipulation and continuous distraction rather than bursts of traction minimize risk of iatrogenic injury (fracture)

    Anterior dislocation
    • Allis technique (superior): patient supine
      • Distal traction to bring head to the level of the acetabulum
      • IR and extension of the hip to reduce femoral head
    • Allis technique (inferior): patient supine
      • Distal traction with gentle hip flexion
      • IR and adduction are combined with lateral force applied to inner thigh

    Posterior dislocation
    • Allis technique: patient supine
      • Assistant stabilizes pelvis with downward force on the anterior superior iliac spine bilaterally
      • Hip and knee flexion relaxes the hamstrings
      • Continuous longitudinal traction is applied
      • Increase traction force while increasing flexion to 70° and applying gentle internal and external rotations to lower extremity
      • Lateral force applied to the thigh by the assistant can be helpful
    • Stimson gravity technique: patient is prone with affected extremity hanging off the end of the stretcher
      • Pelvis is stabilized on the table by an assistant
      • Knee is flexed to 90°
      • Downward-directed force is applied to the posterior calf
      • Gentle rotation may assist reduction
    • Bigelow maneuver: with patient is supine
      • Assistant stabilizes pelvis with downward force on the anterior superior iliac spine bilaterally
      • Continuous longitudinal traction is applied
      • Hip is flexed to 90° with IR and adduction to clear the acetabulum
      • ER and abduction completes reduction
      • High risk for iatrogenic femoral head fractures

  • Post-reduction: radiographs and CT should be obtained to confirm concentric reduction and absence of incarcerated bone fragments
    • Stability of reduction should be tested by flexing hip to 90° in neutral rotation
    • WBAT with appropriate hip precautions based on direction of dislocation if stable without fractures
    • Consider a hip abduction brace for instability in the setting of prosthetic hip

  • Open reduction:
    • To be performed when concentric, closed reduction cannot be achieved, concomitant fracture of the femoral head and/or acetabulum that requires open reduction or when there is an ipsilateral femoral neck fracture
    • Approach depends on location of associated fracture(s):
      • Posterior approach (Kocher-Langenbeck) will allow access to posterior acetabulum
      • Anterior approach (Smith-Peterson) is often recommended for femoral head fractures
        - Avoid removal of the capsule from the neck to prevent injury to the deep MFCA
      • Anterolateral approach (Watson-Jones) is useful for anterior dislocations and femoral head fractures
      • Lateral approach (Hardinge) allows for anterior and posterior exposure through the same incision
        - Avoid striping off tissue from both anterior and posterior to maintain viability
    • Postoperatively patients should be placed on a short period of bed rest followed by protected weight-bearing for 4-6 weeks following reduction

Complications
  • Heterotopic ossification: 2% of patients
  • Neurovascular injury: 10% of dislocations suffer sciatic nerve injury
    • Femoral neurovascular structures are at risk with anterior dislocations
  • Osteonecrosis of the femoral head: < 10% of injuries
    • Risk increases with duration of dislocation (> 6 hours)
    • Can present up to 5 years after injury
    • May be increased by increased attempts at relocation
  • Posttraumatic arthritis: most common
    • Higher with acetabular fractures and femoral head fractures
  • Recurrent dislocation: rare
