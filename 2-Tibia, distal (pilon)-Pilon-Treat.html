Treatment
  • Based on age, functional status, fracture severity, articular involvement, soft tissue envelope and osteoporosis
  • Articular reconstruction can be challenging or not possible
  • Patients should be advised about the increased risk of posttraumatic arthritis
  • Open wounds should be cleansed and dressed in the acute setting
  • Acutely reduce gross displacement of the ankle with traction and reversal of deformity followed by application of a long leg splint (posterior with stir-up) with ankle at 90°

  • Nonoperative: reserved for nondisplaced fractures and severely ill patients
    • LLC for 6 weeks with ankle at 90°
    • Closed reduction is rarely possible and loss of reduction is common
    • Disadvantage: does not allow for soft tissue assessment

  • Operative: indications include displaced fractures if the articular surface is amenable to reconstruction and open fractures
    • Goals: maintenance of fibular length and stability, restoration of the tibial articular surface, bone grafting metaphyseal defects and fixation of fracture fragments
    • Delaying definitive articular fixation until soft tissues are optimized (up to 21 days in severe cases) can decrease the risk of wound complications
      • Provisional calcaneal traction pin or external fixation of the tibia with possible ORIF of the fibula can be performed to maintain length

    Internal fixation: low profile (decreases skin tension) medial, anterior, anterolateral and/or posterior tibial plates can be used in combination depending on fracture pattern
    • Cancellous bone graft, demineralized bone matrix or cement to fill metaphyseal defects
    • 7-10 cm skin bridge between tibial and fibular incision is desired to avoid skin necrosis
    • Minimally invasive percutaneous plate osteosynthesis (MIPPO) utilizes smaller incisions to slide a contoured plate under the soft tissue envelope along tibial metaphysis
      • Percutaneous screw placement reduces surgical involvement of the soft tissues

    External fixation: appropriate for severe soft tissue compromise or open fractures
    • Reduction through traction and ligamentotaxis
    • Inclusion of foot prevents equinus deformity and eliminates soft tissue motion
    • Alternatively, a hybrid fixator (external fixator construct proximally connected to ring fixator around an articular fracture) or ring fixator that excludes the foot can be used to allow early ROM
    • Can be used as definitive treatment if adequate reduction is achieved
    • External fixation with plate fixation of the fibula may be susceptible to varus malalignment

Complications
  • Compartment syndrome: up to 5%
    • Require urgent fasciotomies to release pressure and avoid muscle death
  • Infection: associated with open injuries and soft tissue devitalization
    • Osteomyelitis: can develop in patients with significant wound complications
  • Malunion: commonly due to nonanatomic reduction, inadequate buttressing followed by collapse, or premature weight-bearing
  • Nonunion (5%): increased risk with high comminution, bone loss or infection
    • Lack of union at 12 weeks warrants bone grafting
  • Posttraumatic arthritis: a consequence of any articular injury, can be theoretically minimized with anatomic reduction
    • Arthrodesis can be performed as a salvage procedure
  • Stiffness: due to posttraumatic fibrosis and arthrosis
  • Wound complications: common
    • Highest for Rüedi-Allgöwer type 3 injuries
    • Delayed surgical treatment to allow for soft tissue optimization, closing skin without tension and meticulous soft tissue technique is believed to reduce this risk
    • Secondary skin grafts and/or flaps may be necessary for adequate closure
