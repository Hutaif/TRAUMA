Facts
  • Generally arise from high-energy trauma such as MVC and pedestrian injuries
  • Fracture in the elderly can result from a fall from standing

  • Pelvic ring is formed by the sacrum posteriorly and two innominate bones
    • Innominate bones are formed by the ilium, ischium and pubis
    • The confluence of these three bones forms the acetabulum
      • The ilium forms the large superior portion of the acetabulum and the pelvic wing
      • Ischium forms the posteroinferior portion of the acetabulum and the bony prominence on which people sit
      • Pubis forms the anterior wall of the acetabulum and pubic rami
    • Innominate bones join anteriorly at the pubic symphysis
    • Innominate bones join the sacrum posteriorly at the immobile sacroiliac joints
    • Sacrum connects the pelvis to the spine at L5
      • Represents the fusion of 5 sacral vertebrae
      • 4 anterior and posterior neural foramina on each side
    • Pelvic ring injuries (fracture or joint diastasis) always occur in two places, anterior and posterior
  • Sciatic notch: formed by ilium and contains 7 nerves, 3 sets of vessels and the piriformis muscle
    • Nerves: superior gluteal (above piriformis), sciatic, inferior gluteal, internal pudendal, posterior femoral cutaneous nerve, nerve to the quadratus femoris, nerve to obturator externus
    • Vessels (artery and vein): superior gluteal (above piriformis), inferior gluteal, internal pudendal
  • Ligaments confer stability:
    • Sacroiliac (SI) ligaments: connect sacrum to ilium
      • Divided into long/short posterior (more important for stability) and anterior
      • Provide rotational (short posterior/anterior) and vertical (long posterior) stability to pelvic ring
    • Iliolumbar ligaments: connect L4/5 transverse processes and posterior iliac crest
      • Provide rotation stability
    • Lumbosacral ligaments: connect L5 transverse processes to the sacrum
      • Provide vertical stability
    • Sacrotuberous ligaments: connects sacrum to ischial tuberosities
      • Provide vertical stability
    • Sacrospinous ligaments: connects sacrum to ischial spines
      • Provides rotational stability
    • Pubic symphysis
      • Provides anterior stability
  • Deforming forces:
    • Anteroposterior:
      • Results in ER of hemipelvis, hinging on intact posterior ligaments
    • Lateral compression: most common
      • Results in hemipelvis IR with compression through SI joint and sacrum
    • Vertical shear force: occurs in young where bone strength is greater than ligament strength
      • Upward force applied through innominate bone causes disruption of symphyseal, sacroiliac, sacrospinous and sacrotuberous ligaments
      • Pelvic injuries may result in leg length discrepancy, rule out extremity injuries
  • Corona mortis: retropubic anastomosis of the inferior epigastric/external iliac system with obturator vessels
    • Can be a site of unexplained hemorrhage
    • Often injured by superior public rami fractures
  • L5 nerve root crosses the anterosuperior border of the S1 vertebra and sacral ala
  • Anterior rami of S2 - S5 contribute parasympathetic fibers that control sexual, bladder and rectal function
    • S2: anal sphincter
    • S4-S5: innervate penis and labial sensation
    • S2-S4: innervate the bladder and rectum
    • Unilateral S2-S3: required for continence

  • Patients typically present after high-energy trauma
  • A full trauma resuscitation and evaluation and should be performed
    • Life threatening injuries are first priority
    • Hemodynamic monitoring is required
      • Disruption of the posterior pelvic venous plexus, the superior gluteal artery, median sacral artery or internal/external iliac artery may necessitate fluid resuscitation
      • Immediate control can be achieved by closing the pelvic volume through circumferential sheet wrapping or anterior external fixation
    • Associated injuries include bladder (20%) and urethra (10%)
      • Urethral injuries may indicated by blood at the urethral meatus or inability to pass urinary catheter
      • Retrograde urethrogram for high clinical suspicion of urethral injury
      • Bladder ruptures can be identified by cystogram
  • Examine skin (including perineum, rectum and vagina) with particular attention to wounds that could indicate open fracture (< 5%)
    • Mortality rate for open fractures (25%-due to hemorrhage) higher than closed fractures (18%-due to head trauma)
      • Rectal, vaginal and perineal lacerations suggest contaminated open fracture
      • Blood on rectal examination suggests intestinal injury
    • Flank and buttock contusions may indicate massive pelvic bleeding
      • Associated with unstable fractures
    • Destot's sign: superficial hematoma above inguinal ligament, in scrotum or proximal thigh suggests pelvic fracture
    • Morel-Lavallée lesion: internal degloving injury leading to a serosanguinous fluid filled cavity
      • Affected area (usually over the greater trochanter) demonstrates swelling, fluctuance and ecchymosis
      • Associated with a high rate of infection (up to 33%)
      • Requires aggressive debridement and drainage
  • Anteroposterior compression test (downward force applied to both anterior superior iliac crests) and lateral compression test (medially directed force on both iliac crests) test pelvic stability
    • Should be performed as few times as possible to minimize disruption of clotting
  • Neurologic injury can occur to L2 - S5
    • L5 and S1 are most common
    • 25% of sacral fractures have neurologic injury
      • Check perianal dermatomes (S2-S5) and rectal tone (S2-S4)
      • Bulbocavernosus reflex (S2-S4): anal sphincter contraction with squeezing of the glans penis or pulling of a Foley catheter
      • Cemasteric reflexes (L1-L2): contraction of the cremaster muscle with stroking of the ipsilateral inner thigh
