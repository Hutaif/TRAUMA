Treatment
  • Fight bite: injury to the hand from opponent's teeth
    • Most commonly involves MCP joint
    • Bacterial inoculation occurs with tooth penetration of a closed fist
    • When the first is opened, bacteria are dragged through the full excursion of the tendon into the dorsum of the hand
    • Late presentations and obvious infections require open debridement
    • Acute presentations require wound irrigation and close monitoring
    • Must consider joint contamination with oral flora (S. Aureus, Streptococcus and Eikenella) and treat with broad spectrum antibiotics

  • General fracture treatment principles:
    • Simple fractures should be reduced under digital or hematoma block
    • Reductions can typically be achieved with longitudinal traction and pressure to reverse the deformity
    • Stable fractures in acceptable alignment: generally candidates for nonoperative treatment
      • Fingers: buddy tape (middle/distal phalanx), finger splint (phalanx) or forearm-based splint (metapcarpal)
        - Maintain splint in protected position (intrinsic plus): 0° of P/DIP flexion, 70° of MCP flexion, 20° of wrist extension
        - Minimizes stiffness by placing intrinsics in maximal stretch during immobilization
      • Thumb: spica splint
      • Uninvolved joints should undergo early ROM
      • Early radiographic follow up is required to identify any displacement
    • Unstable fractures or unacceptable alignment: generally require operative treatment
      • Splint acutely followed by operative fixation
      • Percutaneous pin: allow fixation with minimal dissection
        - Loss of reduction, tethering of soft tissues and infection are concerns
      • Lag screw: allows fracture compression without irritating profile of plate
      • Plate: allow precise reduction and compression of the fracture
        - Tendon adherence and larger exposure are concerns
        - Generally reserved for complex articular fractures, multiple fractures or associated soft tissue injuries
      • Postoperative splinting in the protected position is critical to minimize stiffness

  • Fracture treatment by location:
    Metacarpal:
    • Head fractures:
      • Stable reductions can be treated nonoperatively using a splint in the protected position (see above)
      • Operative: indicated for incongruity of the articular surface > 1 mm or unstable fractures
        - Lag screws are generally preferred

    • Neck fractures:
      • Generally can be treated nonoperatively after closed reduction
      • Acceptable alignment:
        - Thumb: < 20° angulation
        - 2nd and 3rd MC: < 15°
        - 4th and 5th MC (Boxer's): < 45°
      • Operative: indications include unacceptable alignment and unstable reductions
        - Retrograde intramedullary K-wires
        - Transverse pinning: three wires placed through adjacent intact MC, acting as an external fixator

    • Shaft fractures:
      • Acceptable alignment:
        - Thumb: < 20° angulation
        - 2nd and 3rd MC: < 10° angulation
        - 4th and 5th MC: < 20° angulation
      • Operative: indications include unacceptable alignment and unstable fractures
        - Transverse or short oblique: transverse pinning to the adjacent MC
        - Long oblique: lag screw
        - Comminuted: plate

    • Base of thumb metacarpal fractures:
      • Extraarticular: alignment is less crucial due to mobility of the CMC joint
        - Minimally displaced fractures can be treated by closed reduction/thumb spica casting
        - Displaced fractures may require pinning or screw fixation
      • Intraarticular: restoration of alignment is critical to minimize arthrosis
        - Both Bennett's and Rolando's fractures are generally treated with CRPP or ORIF (see Carpometacarpal Dislocation chapter)

    • Base of finger metacarpal fractures:
      • 2nd, 3rd and 4th are generally minimally displaced: volar resting splint followed by early ROM
      • Reverse Bennett's fracture: fracture-dislocation of base of 5th/hamate
        - MC pulled proximally by extensor carpi ulnaris (ECU)
        - Radiographs: fracture best visualized with 30° pronated view
        - Often requires ORIF with lag screws

    Proximal and middle phalanges:
    • CRPP is preferred when fixation is required due to decreased trauma to gliding structures of the hand
    • Extraarticular:
      • Middle phalanx fractures at the base angle dorsally while neck fractures angle volarly due to pull of FDS tendon
      • Attempt closed reduction with finger trap traction
      • Acceptable alignment:
        - No rotational deformity
        - Angulation < 10°
      • Operative: indicated for unacceptable alignment or instability
        - Transverse: retrograde intramedullary K-wires
        - Long oblique: transverse K-wires or lag screws
        - Short oblique: lag screws
        - Comminuted: plate fixation

    • Head fractures:
      • Require anatomic reduction and fixation for displacement > 1 mm
        - K-wire or lag screw
      • Comminuted: requires reconstruction of articular surface
        - Laterally positioned contoured plate is preferred and allows early motion
        - If not reconstructable, treat closed

    • Base fractures:
      • Reduce joint with axial traction and manual pressure to correct deformity
      • Volar lip:
        - Articular involvement < 30%: extension block splinting (dorsal splint with 20° of IP flexion, allowing flexion but prevents extension)
        - Articular involvement > 30%: open reduction and pin fixation or volar plate arthroplasty
      • Dorsal lip:
        - Consider central slip avulsion fracture
        - Fractures with < 1 mm displacement: may be treated with splint in extension (like Boutonnière injury)
        - Fractures with > 1 mm displacement or volar subluxation: open fixation
      • Highly commminuted fractures can be treated with dynamic traction using K-wires and dental rubber bands
        - Allows early motion and remodeling of an otherwise nonreconstructable articular surface

    Distal phalanx:
    • Extraarticular:
      • Comminuted fractures often also have nailbed injuries (see below)
      • Closed reduction followed by finger splint immobilization is suitable for most injuries
      • Wide displacement can be treated with intramedullary pinning

    • Intraarticular:
      • Dorsal lip
        - Mallet finger may develop from injury to the extensor tendon
        - Full-time extension splint for 6-8 weeks is appropriate in most fractures
        - Involvement of > 25% of the articular surface or joint subluxation can be treated with closed reduction and pinning or ORIF
      • Volar lip
        - Associated with Jersey finger (FDP rupture, commonly ring finger)
        - ORIF is preferred, especially for large displaced intraarticular fragments
        - Closed reduction and extension block pinning can also be considered for smaller fragments

    • Nailbed injuries:
      • Evacuate subungual hematomas via trephination
      • If nail has been avulsed, it should be cleansed and retained for a biologic dressing
      • Suture nailbed lacerations with 7-0 chromic under magnification

  • Dislocation treatment by location
    Metacarpalphalangeal, finger:
    • Closed reduction:
      • Dorsal dislocation: flex wrist and PIP to relax flexors then apply volarly directed pressure to dorsal aspect of proximal phalanx with longitudinal traction
      • Volar dislocation: flex MCP and apply dorsally directed pressure to volar aspect of proximal phalanx while extending digit
      • Test stability after reduction
    • Nonoperative: stable reductions can be treated with buddy taping and early ROM
      • Instability post-reduction requires a splint in 50° of MCP flexion for 3 weeks
    • Operative: indications include irreducible dislocations, volar dislocations and fracture-dislocations
      • Open reductions can be performed through either a volar (puts neurovascular bundle at risk) or a dorsal approach
      • Volar dislocations benefit from volar plate repair to prevent late re-dislocation
      • Fracture-dislocations can be treated with percutaneous pinning or ORIF

    Metacarpalphalangeal, thumb:
    • Closed reduction:
      • Dorsal dislocation: flex MCP and apply longitudinal traction
      • Evaluate stability by ranging the joint
      • Stress radiographs may be useful in diagnosing ulnar instability
      • Stener lesion can be diagnosed when a palpable tender mass is present along the ulnar aspect of the MCP joint
    • Nonoperative: indicated for most dislocations
      • Spica splint with MCP in mild flexion for 3 weeks
    • Operative: indications include irreducible dislocations, wide ulnar instabiltiy and Stener lesions
      • Repair of the ulnar collateral ligament can be performed with sutures or suture anchors
      • Bony avulsions can be repaired with wires or sutures

    Proximal interphalangeal:
    • Closed reduction: longitudinal traction and manual pressure to reverse deformity
    • Nonoperative:
      • Dorsal dislocation: splint finger in extension for 10 days followed by ROM exercises
      • Volar dislocation: extension splinting of the PIP for 4-6 weeks
        - DIP should be left free to perform flexion exercises
    • Operative: indications include irreducible dislocations and open dislocations
      • A mid-axial approach allows treatment of both volar and dorsal dislocations
      • Repair of the radial collateral ligament of the index finger can be considered

    Distal and thumb interphalangeal:
    • Closed reduction can be achieved with axial traction and pressure to reverse the deformity
    • Test stability by ranging the joint
    • Stable reductions can begin active motion immediately
    • Unstable reductions should be immobilized for 2-3 weeks in 20° of flexion
    • Highly unstable joints may require percutaneous pin fixation


Complications
  • Joint stiffness: associated with all dislocations as well as plate and screw fixation and prolonged immobilization
    • High-energy injuries with comminution and intraarticular involvement predispose to stiffness
    • Reduce risk with early mobilization and with splinting in the protected position (70° of flexion at MCP)
  • Malunion: rotational, angular deformities, especially of the 2nd and 3rd MCs may result in functional and cosmetic disturbance
  • Nonunion: transverse MC fractures treated with plate fixation are at highest risk
    • Lag screw fixation may increase healing rate
  • Percutaneous pin fixation: infection (7%), pin loosening (5%) and nonunion (4%)
  • Residual instability: associated with fracture-dislocations
