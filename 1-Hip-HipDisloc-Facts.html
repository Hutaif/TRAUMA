Facts
  • Most commonly sustained during MVC
    • 90% occur on the right, possibly due to leg position while engaging brake pedal

  • 40% of femoral head is covered by the acetabulum
  • Hip joint has inherent stability due to bony and soft tissue anatomy
    • Fibrocartilanginous labrum deepens the acetabulum, increasing coverage and stability
    • Thick capsule and ligaments (iliofemoral, pubofemoral and ischiofemoral) provide further support
    • Soft tissue structures including the labrum, iliofemoral ligament, capsule, bony fragments and tendons (piriformis, psoas) can prevent closed reduction
  • Adult femoral head blood supply from:
    • Deep branch of the medial femoral circumflex artery (MFCA): supplies majority of weight-bearing superior head
    • Ligamentum teres (branch of the obturator artery): supplies about 10%
    • Lateral femoral circumflex artery (LFCA): minor contribution
    • MFCA and LFCA arise from the profunda femoris artery

    Posterior dislocation (85%):
    • Patients present with a shortened, flexed, internally rotated and adducted lower extremity
    • The result of a posterior force on a flexed hip
    • Acetabular fractures occur if hip is in relative abduction
    • Femoral head fractures complicate 5-15% of posterior hip dislocations
    • Sciatic nerve injury occurs in 10% as it exits the pelvis at the greater sciatic notch deep to the piriformis
      • Peroneal divsion more commonly affected than tibial: test tibialis anterior, EHL and peroneals

    Anterior dislocation (15%):
    • Patients present with an externally rotated and abducted lower extremity
    • Associated with femoral vessel and femoral head indentation injuries

    Inferior/obturator: the result of simultaneous abduction, ER, flexion

    Superior: the result of simultaneous abduction, ER, extension

  • Hip dislocations represent an orthopedic emergency requiring prompt reduction to protect femoral head blood supply
  • High-energy injury nature warrants a full trauma evaluation and resuscitation
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
  • Other injuries are common due to the high-energy nature including: abdominal, chest, acetabular, and spine
  • 50% of patients have fractures
    • Almost all femoral head fractures are associated with hip dislocations
