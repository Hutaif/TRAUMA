Treatment
  • Nonoperative: indicated for minimally displaced fractures (< 1 cm of translation and 45° of angulation), valgus impacted two-part surgical neck fractures or fracture-dislocations of the greater tuberosity that reduce following concentric reduction of the shoulder joint (most GT fractures reduce with closed reduction of the joint)
    • Sling immobilization followed by early ROM at 2 weeks
    • Elbow and hand mobilization should begin immediately

  • Operative: indications include angulation > 45°, displacement > 1 cm (5 mm for greater tuberosity) or fracture-dislocations
    • Objective is to restore articular and tuberosity anatomy while minimizing vascular supply to the humeral head
    • Fixation options include closed reduction/percutaneous pinning (CRPP) or ORIF with transosseous sutures, conventional plate fixation or locked plate fixation
    • CRPP is most appropriate for two-part surgical neck fractures in non-osteoporotic patients
    • Locked plates with fixed angle screws are preferred for osteoporotic bone and metaphyseal comminution
    • Most fractures can be fixed via a deltapectoral or deltoid splitting (for GT fracture) approach
    • Minimize soft tissue dissection along medial metaphysis to reduce vascular disruption to the humeral head
    • Hemiarthroplasty is appropriate for marked articular comminution

    Two-part:
  • Anatomic neck: high rate of osteonecrosis
    • ORIF or hemiarthroplasty in older patients
  • Surgical neck: valgus impacted patterns do well nonoperatively
    • Closed reduction should be attempted followed by stabilization by percutaneous pinning if acceptable alignment is achieved
    • Irreducible or complex/comminuted fractures can be fixed with proximal humeral plates
  • Greater tuberosity fracture: screw or suture fixation ± cuff repair
    • Can be accessed via a superior deltoid splitting approach
    • Arthroscopic fixation can allows evaluation of the labrum and cartilage while avoiding deltoid dissection
  • Lesser tuberosity fracture: isolated displaced fractures are rare
    • Can be treated with excision and subscapularis advancement or screw fixation

    Three-part: generally ORIF is preferred with suture fixation of tuberosity fragments and locking plate fixation of the metaphysis
  • Bone grafting may be required to buttress impacted segments
  • Hemiarthroplasty should be considered for elderly patients with poor bone quality

    Four-part: high incidence of osteonecrosis
  • CRPP or ORIF can be considered in valgus impacted fractures
  • When valgus impaction is not present ORIF can be considered for high-demand patients or patients < 40 years
  • Otherwise hemiarthroplasty is the preferred treatment

    Impression: treated like reverse Hill-Sachs lesion
  • Defect < 20% of articular surface: transfer of the lesser tuberosity and subscapularis into defect
  • Defect < 40%: osteochondral allograft or bone grafting
  • Defect > 40%: hemiarthroplasty

    Fracture-dislocation:
  • Two part fracture-dislocations of the greater or lesser tuberosities may reduce after closed shoulder reduction and can be treated nonoperatively (see above)
  • For fractures involving the anatomic or surgical neck:
    • ORIF can be considered for younger patients
    • Hemiarthroplasty is generally recommended for advanced age, poor bone stock or severe comminution

Complications
  • Malunion: often due to failed ORIF or nonanatomic reduction
    • Varus deformity (collapse) usually the result of medial metaphyseal comminution
    • Associated with poor functional outcomes
    • Corrective osteotomy can be considered
  • Nerve injuries: brachial plexus and axillary nerve injuries
  • Nonunion: seen with two part surgical neck fractures due to soft tissue interposition
  • Osteonecrosis: 3-14% of three-part and 13-34% of four-part fractures
    • Risk increases with displacement
    • Leads to posttraumatic arthritis
    • Anatomic reconstruction may allow sufficient perfusion by posteromedial vessels
    • Symptomatic cases can be treated with hemiarthroplasty
  • Shoulder stiffness: increased risk with arthroplasty to treat fracture-dislocation
    • Associated with residual greater tuberosity displacement (subacromial impingement)
    • Can be a result of capsular contracture
    • Avoid by initiating early passive shoulder motion
  • Posttraumatic arthritis: a consequence of any articular injury, can be theoretically minimized with anatomic reduction
  • Vascular injuries: axillary artery is at risk, requires vascular surgery consultation
