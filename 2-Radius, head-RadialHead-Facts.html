Facts
  • Usually occur after a fall on an outstretched arm
  • Associated with elbow dislocations and ligamentous injuries of the elbow, particularly medial collateral ligament (MCL) following a valgus load

  • Lateral collateral ligament (LCL) consists of the radial collateral ligament, the lateral ulnar collateral ligament (LUCL) and the annular ligament
    • Provides varus and posterolateral rotatory stability
    • LUCL is most important
  • MCL consists of the anterior and posterior bundle as well as the transverse ligament
    • Provides valgus and posteromedial rotatory stability
    • Anterior bundle most important
  • Radial head is intraarticular
    • Articulates with the lesser sigmoid notch of the ulna and capitellum of the distal humerus
    • Lateral aspect of the radial head is not covered by cartilage because it does not articulate with anything
  • Functional elbow motion: 20°-120° of flexion, 70° of pronation and supination
  • Radial head is an important secondary stabilizer of the elbow to valgus stress
    • Shares 50% of the load at the elbow
    • Prevents proximal migration of the radius
  • Interosseous membrane (IOM) is important for longitudinal stability
    • Middle third is the strongest
  • Essex-Lopresti lesion: radial head fracture, distal radioulnar joint dislocation (DRUJ) and interosseous membrane (IOM) disruption
    • DRUJ dislocation is usually dorsal
    • Important to recognize, particularly if radial head excision is being considered (radius can migrate proximally due to longitudinal instability)
    • May result in loss of forearm rotation and extension
    • DRUJ tenderness is most sensitive test

  • Patients present with elbow pain, variable swelling and ecchymosis
  • Often demonstrate tenderness to palpation over the radial head and pain or crepitation with supination/pronation
  • Blocks to rotation and elbow effusion may be present
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
  • DRUJ must be examined for Essex-Lopresti lesion
    • Complaints of DRUJ or mid-forearm pain should raise suspicion for IOM disruption
  • MCL disruption, elbow dislocations, capitellum, olecranon and coronoid fractures are common associated injuries
    • MCL injury should be suspected by tenderness to palpation along the medial elbow
