Treatment
  • Advanced Trauma Life Support protocols should be followed in any patient that is hemodynamically unstable
    • Initial orthopedic intervention aimed at stabilizing pelvis to control hemorrhage
    • Pelvic binder or circumferential sheet wrapping can achieve temporary stabilization in APC injuries
    • External fixation can be applied quickly to the anterior iliac crest and is appropriate for APC II injuries
    • Ongoing hypotension may be a sign of persistent pelvic bleeding: angiographic embolization should be considered
      • Usually due to branches of the internal iliac artery
    • Open fractures associated with high mortality rates
      • Require aggressive irrigation and debridement
      • Colonic injuries may require diverting colostomy

  • Damage control orthopedics: in polytrauma patients, initial interventions should provide expedient stabilization of long bone and pelvic fractures (e.g. external fixation)
    • Definitive procedures should be delayed until the patient's general condition has stabilized
    • Minimizes the "second hit" associated with physiological burden of surgery in patients at risk of multi-organ failure and acute respiratory distress
    • Patients at risk typically demonstrate hemodynamic instability, difficult resuscitation, polytrauma, coagulopathy and hypothermia
    • Immunological markers such as IL-6 can be used to monitor inflammatory response of at risk patients

  • Dynamic stress examinations under anesthesia may be required to identify pelvic instability

  • Pelvic fixation techniques:
    Percutanous fixation: indicated in unstable pelvic injuries with minimal displacement
    • Includes external fixation, medullary pubic ramus screws and sacral iliac screws

    External fixation: typically rectangular construct consisting of two pins in each anterior iliac crest (must be intact) either as temporary or definitive treatment
    • LC: treatment for 4-6 weeks with gradual mobilization
    • APC: treatment for 8-12 weeks depending on status of posterior sacroiliac ligaments
    • Vertical shear: treatment for 12 weeks

    Internal fixation: stronger resistance to forces than external fixation
    • Iliac wing fracture: interfragmentary compression and neutralization with pelvic reconstruction plates
    • Public symphysis diastasis > 3.5 cm: symphyseal plate
    • Sacral fractures: plate fixation or sacroiliac screws
    • Complete sacroiliac disruption: plate fixation or sacroiliac screws

  • Treatment by injury location:
    Anterior:
    • Pubic symphysis diastasis:
      • Nonoperative: indicated for < 2.5 cm of diastasis or stable posterior ring
        - Protected weight-bearing and symptomatic treatment
      • Operative: > 2.5 cm of diastasis or posterior ring instability
        - External fixation, symphyseal plate (Pfannenstiel/Stoppa approach) or posterior ring stabilization
    • Public rami fractures:
      • Nonoperative: generally indicated in the setting of posterior ring stability
        - Protected weight-bearing and symptomatic treatment
      • Operative: indications include rotationally unstable injuries
        - External fixation, plate fixation or retrograde medullary screws

    Posterior:
    • Iliac fractures:
      • Nonoperative: indicated for minimally displaced, stable fractures
        - Protected weight-bearing and symptomatic treatment
      • Operative: indications include severely displaced, unstable, comminuted or open fractures
        - Internal fixation with compression screws and reconstruction plates
    • Sacroiliac joint:
      • Disruptions of the sacroiliac joint may be associated with partial or complete disruption of the posterior ligament complex
      • Nonoperative: indicated for stable injuries with intact posterior SI ligament
        - Protected weight-bearing and symptomatic treatment
      • Operative: indications include unstable, displaced injuries
        - Anterior or posterior sacroiliac plating or sacroiliac screws
        - Should be performed in conjunction with stabilization of the anterior pelvic ring
        - Risk of injury to L5 and upper sacral nerve roots
    • Sacral fractures:
      • Nonoperative: indicated for stable, nondisplaced and neurologically intact injuries and fractures not involving the lumbosacral junction
        - TDWB for 8 weeks
      • Operative: indications include displaced (> 1 cm), unstable patterns or osseous nerve impingement
        - ORIF with posterior tension banding, percutaneous iliosacral screws or transiliac sacral bars
        - Foraminal decompression is required for neurologic injuries

  • Associated acetabular fractures are treated prior to pelvic ring stabilization
  • Early mobilization is desired and thromboembolic prophylaxis should be provided

Complications
  • Deep vein thrombosis (13-31%): immobilization is a major risk factor
    • Pulmonary embolism (3-13%): up to 3% are fatal
    • Inferior vena cava filter should be considered for unstable injuries
  • Gastrointestinal injury: must treat as open fracture and perform aggressive irrigation and debridement
  • Genitourinary injury (10-20%): more common in males
    • Bladder rupture is a marker of severe trauma
  • Heterotopic ossification (8%)
  • Infection: up to 25%
    • Careful debridement of dead space and hematomas can reduce risk
  • Malunion: significant disability can occur with chronic pain, leg length discrepancies, gait disturbance and difficulty sitting
    • Associated with vertical shear patterns and patient noncompliance
  • Nerve injury (10%): associated with displacement of posterior ring injury and sacral fractures
    • Motor deficits occur most commonly with Tile C injuries
    • 80% of neurologic deficits associated with sacral fracture improve regardless of treatment
  • Thromoembolic disease: Up to 35% of all patients will develop in calf, thigh or pelvis
    • Requires prophylaxis with pharmacologic and/or mechanical means
  • Vascular injury: increased risk with VS, APC II/III and LC III
