Treatment
  • Initial treatment for calcaneus fractures includes cleansing and dressing of any open wounds followed by compression with a bulky Jones dressing to reduce swelling
    • Jones dressing: cotton padding and elastic bandage layered successively two to three times around foot and ankle
    • Delay surgery for up to 3 weeks to allow for sufficient healing of the soft tissues to reduce risk of postoperative wound complications
      • Clear fluid-filled blisters retain epithelial cells and re-epithelialize quicker
      • Blood filled blisters represent more significant injury and do not retain epithelial cells

  • Nonoperative: indicated for extraarticular or nondisplaced posterior facet fractures, highly comminuted fractures in osteopenic bone or calcaneocuboid joint involvement < 25%
    • NWB in a splint followed by transition to a fracture boot, range of motion exercises and progressive weight-bearing
    • NWB is required for approximately 10 weeks
    • Essex-Lopresti maneuver: reduction of tongue-type fractures can be achieved with percutaneous Steinmann pin manipulation
    • Due to increased risk of wound complications, some diabetics and smokers may be better served with nonoperative treatment

  • Operative: indications include displaced posterior facet or open fractures, calcaneocuboid involvement > 25%
    • Thorough irrigation and debridement of open wounds is necessary
    • Restore congruity of the subtalar articulation, Böhler's angle, neutralize varus alignment, calcaneocuboid articulation and normal height/width of calcaneus
    • ORIF through lateral L incision allows direct visualization of the subtalar joint
      • Protect sural nerve
      • Reduce anterior process, posterior tuberosity, posterior facet, lateral wall and buttress with lateral plate
      • Medial approach allows direct visualization of sutentaculum tali but risks neurovascular structures
    • Primary subtalar arthrodesis is generally not recommended but may be appropriate for Sanders IV fractures
    • Early subtalar ROM is important to minimize stiffness
    • Typical time to weight-bearing is 3 months

    Anterior process: generally nonoperative
    • Small symptomatic fragments can be excised
    • Larger (> 1 cm) displaced fragments should be fixed

    Tuberosity: significant displacement should be urgently treated with open reduction to avoid skin necrosis

Complications
  • Chronic pain: more common in work related injuries
  • Compartment syndrome (10%): urgent fasciotomy is required to release pressure and avoid muscle death
  • Increased heel width: may result in lateral impingement on the peroneal tendons or fibula
  • Posttraumatic arthritis: subtalar or calcaneocuboid
    • May require salvage arthrodesis but due to the coupled motion of the hindfoot joints (subtalar, calcaneocuboid, talonavicular), this essentially eliminates motion at the remaining joints
  • Stiffness: most patients lose up to 50% of their subtalar motion despite rehabilitation
  • Sural neuropathy: associated with lateral approach
  • Wound dehiscence: most common at the angle of incision
    • Frequently leads to calcaneal osteomyelitis
    • Minimize occurrence by delaying surgery until soft tissues edema has resolved
    • Risk factors include high body mass index, open fractures, diabetes and smoking
