Facts
  • Most common long bone fracture
  • Typical mechanisms include MVC (younger age groups), falls (elderly) and sport related

  • Tibia is triangular in cross section and subcutaneous along its anteromedial border
  • Blood supply mainly from posterior tibial artery which enters the posterolateral cortex near the soleus origin and feeds the endosteal vascular tree
  • Anterior tibial artery supplies periosteal blood vessels
    • Vulnerable as it passes through the interosseous membrane after branching off the popliteal artery
  • Popliteal vessels emerge from adductor hiatus to enter the popliteal fossa posteriorly
    • Five geniculate branches
    • Proceeds under soleus through a fibrous tunnel
    • Trifurcation into posterior tibial, peroneal and anterior tibial arteries occur near this point
  • The fibula bears 15% of the axial load
    • The peroneal nerve courses around the fibular neck and is vulnerable in its subcutaneous position
    • Subsequently divides into the deep and superficial peroneal nerves
  • Leg has 4 compartments:
    • Anterior: tibialis anterior, extensor hallucis longus, extensor digitorum longus, peroneus tertius, anterior tibial vessels and deep peroneal nerve
    • Lateral: peroneus longus, peroneus brevis and superficial peroneal nerve
    • Deep posterior: flexor digitorum longus, flexor hallucis longus, tibialis anterior, posterior tibial vessels and the posterior tibial nerve
    • Superficial posterior: gastrocnemius, plantaris and soleus
  • The limited soft tissue envelope around the tibia, particularly the anteromedial surface, predisposes these factures to wound complications
    • Highly traumatized soft tissue, even in the absence of serious wounds, are poor environments for surgical wound healing
  • Relatively poor soft tissue vascularization around this region can complicate wound management and fracture healing
    • Risk of wound complications is increased by conditions such as diabetes or peripheral vascular disease
  • Gastrocnemius can be used as a muscle flap for coverage of large proximal soft tissue defects
  • Soleus can be used for distal third defects
  • Posterior tibial nerve function often important in selecting limb salvage over amputation in severe injuries
  • Fracture pattern and mechanism:
    • High-energy injuries result in transverse fractures or comminuted fractures
    • Bending injuries result in short oblique, transverse or butterfly patterns
    • Crush injuries result in comminution and segmental fractures
    • Torsional injuries result in spiral fractures
  • Stress fractures
    • Military: commonly sustained at the metaphyseal/diaphyseal junction
      • Posteromedial cortex demonstrates most bone reaction, likely due to greater soft tissue coverage
    • Ballet: middle third

  • Patients typically present with pain, deformity and swelling
  • High-energy mechanisms require a full trauma evaluation and resuscitation
    • Given the importance of the soft tissues, understanding the mechanism of injury is important to determine extent of soft tissue injury, particularly when open wounds are not present
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
    • Up to 24% of fractures are open
    • Fibula is not fractured in up to 22% (associated with malunion)
    • Knee ligmament injuries are common
    • High-energy injuries are associated with pelvic and head trauma
    • Fracture blisters and contusions about the tibia may warrant delay of open treatment until soft tissues are more suitable for surgical incisions
  • Compartment syndrome: sustained increase in compartment pressure due to inflammation and edema in one or more of the leg compartments leading to vascular compromise and muscle death
    • Muscle death occurs 6-8 hours after the onset of compartment syndrome
    • Severe pain with passive motion of the toes/foot is often first sign
    • Other signs include tense swelling of the calf, diminished pulses, pain out of proportion to injury and paresthesias
      • Serial neurovascular exams are required as compartment syndrome can develop more than 24 hours after injury
    • Compartment pressures should be measured in patients demonstrating clinical signs of compartment syndrome
      • Compartment pressure > 30 mmHg or within 30 mmHg of the diastolic blood pressure warrant urgent fasciotomies of the leg compartments
    • Deep posterior compartment pressure may be elevated in the presence of a soft superficial compartment
      • Missed posterior compartment syndrome can result in claw toe deformity
