Facts
  • Carpal bone fractures are commonly missed, often diagnosed as "wrist sprain"
  • Scaphoid fractures are most common
  • The carpus is made up of 8 bones grouped into 2 rows
    • Distal carpal row: strong ligamentous connections within the row and with the MCs form a rigid transverse arch
      • Includes trapezium (articulates with 1st MC), trapezoid (2nd MC), capitate (3rd), hamate (4th and 5th)
    • Proximal carpal row: articulates with the radius and ulna and includes the scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum
      • Pisiform is a sesamoid of the flexor carpi ulnaris (FCU) tendon and articulates only with the triquetrum

    Scaphoid
  • Usually the result of a fall onto the radial side of an extended wrist
  • 80% is covered with articular cartilage
  • Represents the deep border of the anatomic snuff box (between EPB and EPL, base of 1st MC and radial styloid)
  • Blood supply from radial artery
    • Perforating branches enter at dorsal ridge and supply proximal 75%
    • Blood supply to the proximal pole is most tenuous
    • Volar contributions from the radial artery supply distal pole
  • Scaphoid spans both proximal and distal carpal rows as a strut
  • Scaphoid rests on radioscapholunate ligament which stabilizes scapholunate articulation and acts as a neurovascular conduit
  • Rotates from volar-flexed perpendicular position (radial deviation of the wrist) to dorsiflexed longitudinal position (ulnar deviation)
  • Flexion across scaphoid is balanced with extension at triquetrum
  • Midcarpal instability:
    • Proximal carpal row acts as "intercalated segment", connecting radius/ulna to a rigid distal row
    • Dorsal intercalated segment instability (DISI): commonly due to scaphoid fracture or scapholunate ligament injury
      • Lunate extends due to loss of support from radius (via the scaphoid)
      • With fractures, proximal fragment of scaphoid tends to extend with lunate
      • Scapholunate angle: > 70°
      • Radiolunate angle: > 10° extension
    • Dorsal intercalated segment instability
  • Patients present with wrist pain and variable swelling and limitation of ROM
  • Snuffbox tenderness in is often present but nonspecific
  • Tenderness at the scaphoid tubercle (distal pole, palpable at the volar wrist crease particularly in radial deviation) suggests fracture
  • Axial compression of the thumb reproduces pain
  • Scaphoid shift (Watson) test: ulnar/radial deviation of the wrist while applying manual pressure on scaphoid tubercle
    • Clunk or pain suggests scapholunate ligament injury

    Lunate
  • Often due to a hyperextension injury
  • Kienböck's disease: osteonecrosis of the lunate
    • Unrecognized fractures can progress to Kienböck's Disease
  • Although often well vascularized by an anastomotic network of dorsal and volar vessels, 20% have only a volar blood supply

  • Patients present with localized tenderness to palpation usually along the volar wrist often with painful ROM

    Triquetrum
  • Commonly associated with ulnar styloid fracture
  • Rich anastomotic vascular network makes nonunion rare
  • Isolated fractures are rare, more commonly associated with perilunate fracture-dislocations

  • Patients present with localized tenderness on the dorsoulnar aspect of the wrist often with painful ROM and decreased grip strength

    Pisiform
  • A sesamoid of the flexor carpi ulnaris (FCU) tendon
  • Articulates only with the triquetrum
  • Forms ulnar border of Guyon's canal (deep: transverse carpal ligament, superfical: pisohamate ligament, radial border: hamate, contents: ulnar artery and nerve)

  • Patients present with tenderness to palpation on the volar aspect of the ulnar wrist often with painful passive extension of the wrist as the FCU is placed under tension

    Trapezium
  • Results from impaction from axial loading of adducted thumb
  • Avulsion of transverse carpal ligament from trapezial ridge may occur

  • Patients present with tenderness to palpation in the radial wrist often with painful ROM of 1st CMC joint

    Trapezoid
  • Fractures are rare, more likely to dislocate

  • Patients present with tenderness to palpation proximal to base of the 2nd MC often with painful ROM of 2nd CMC
  • Dorsal prominence suggests a dislocated trapezoid

    Capitate
  • Isolated injuries are rare
    • Fractures are associated with greater arc perilunate fracture-dislocations
  • Single vessel blood supply

  • Patients present with tenderness and often with painful dorsiflexion of the wrist as the capitate impinges on the dorsal rim of the radius
  • Palpable deformity may be present at the base of the 3rd MC

    Hamate
  • Hamulus or hook projects from the volar surface
    • Forms the ulnar border of the carpal tunnel (deep: carpus, superficial: transverse carpal ligament, radial border: scaphoid/trapezium, contents: FDS, FDP, FCR, FPL and median nerve)
    • Forms radial border of Guyon's canal (deep: transverse carpal ligament, superficial: pisohamate ligament, ulnar border: pisiform, contents: ulnar artery and nerve)
  • Os hamulus proprium: failure of fusion of the hook ossification center
    • Can mimic hook of hamate fractures
  • Origin of flexor digiti minimi brevis and opponens digiti minimi may displace hamate fracture and prevent healing
  • Hook of hamate fractures are common among athletes due to prominence on ulnar wrist, particularly hockey, golf and baseball
  • Distal articular surface fractures are rare and associated with 5th MC subluxation

  • Patients present with pain and tenderness over the hamate
  • Pain can be accentuated with axial loading of the 4th and 5th digits and active grasp
  • Ulnar and median neuropathy may be present as well as ulnar artery injuries
