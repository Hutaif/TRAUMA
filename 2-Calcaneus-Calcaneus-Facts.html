Facts
  • Most frequently fractured tarsal bone
  • Associated with MVCs and falls from height
  • 75% are intraarticular (subtalar)

  • Three facets articulate with the talus
    • Posterior: largest and supports the majority of the weight-bearing
    • Middle: located on the sustentaculum tali
    • Anterior: confluent with the middle
  • Sulcus of the calcaneus (between the middle and posterior facets)
  • Sustentaculum tali: a bony shelf that projects medially from the calcaneal body
    • Articulates with the talus as part of the subtalar joint
    • Supports the neck of the talus medially
    • Flexor hallucis longus passes beneath
    • Attaches to the talus via talocalcaneal ligaments
  • Achilles tendon attaches to the posterior tuberosity (the entire portion of the calcaneus posterior to posterior facet)
  • Anterior process articulates with the cuboid
  • Foot compartments:
    • Medial: abductor hallucis, flexor hallucis brevis
    • Interosseous: 7 interossei muscles
    • Central superficial: flexor digitorum brevis, adductor hallucis, lumbricals
    • Central deep (calcaneal): quadratus plantae
    • Lateral: flexor digiti minimi brevis, abductor digiti minimi, opponens digiti minimi
  • Most intraarticular fractures are the result of axial loading where the talus is driven down in the calcaneus
  • Outcomes from calcaneus fractures tend to be poor due to complex anatomy

  • Patients present with heel pain and variable swelling, widening/shortening of the heel and ecchymosis extending to the arch
  • Mondor's sign: ecchymosis that extends along the outside of the foot to the sole distally
  • Assess skin condition and NV status with particular attention to swelling and open wounds
    • 8% are open fractures
      • Low-velocity open injuries typically occur medially due to sustentaculum fragment
      • Open fractures are associated with higher rates of complications such as infections and amputations
  • Identify associated injuries including spine (10%) and extremity fractures (25%)
    • 7% have contralateral calcaneus fractures
  • Conditions such as diabetes or peripheral vascular disease that predispose to wound healing issues should be identified
  • Failure of treatment is reportedly higher in worker's compensation patients
  • A history of smoking is associated with increased wound complications
  • Compartment syndrome: sustained increase in compartment pressure due to inflammation and edema in one or more of the foot compartments leading to vascular compromise and muscle death
    • Signs include tense swelling, diminished pulses, severe swelling and pain out of proportion to injury, particularly with passive extension of the digits
    • Most commonly involves the central compartment (intrinsic toe flexors)
    • Compartment pressures should be measured when signs of compartment syndrome are present
    • Urgent fasciotomy of the compartments is warranted for pressures within 30 mmHg of the diastolic blood pressure
