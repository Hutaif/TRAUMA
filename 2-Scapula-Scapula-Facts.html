Facts
  • Fractures are usually the result of high-energy trauma
  • Most commonly involve the scapular body (50%) followed by the glenoid fossa (25%) and glenoid neck (10%)

  • Links upper extremity to axial skeleton
    • Acromioclavicular (AC) joint and coracoclavicular (CC) ligaments connect scapula to the clavicle
    • CC ligament has two components: trapezoid (lateral) and conoid (medial)
  • Large muscular envelope protects the scapula
    • 17 muscles insert or originate at the scapula: pectoralis minor, omohyoid, serratus anterior, subscapularis, triceps, biceps (long and short head), coracobrachialis, rhomboid major and minor, infraspinatus, levator scapulae, supraspinatus, trapezius, deltoid, teres minor and major and latissimus dorsi
  • Transverse scapular ligament bridges the scapular notch (medial to base of coracoid)
    • Suprascapular nerve passes under the ligament through the notch
    • Suprascapular artery passes over the ligament
  • Spinoglenoid notch lies at the base of the acromion where it meets the scapular spine
    • Suprascapular nerve passes through this notch prior to innervating the infraspinatus
  • Os acrominale is an unfused acromial apophysis that can be confused with a fracture
    • Present in 3% of the population and is bilateral in 60% of cases
    • Most common site of nonunion is between mesoacromion and metaacromion in the middle of the acromion
  • Superior shoulder suspensory complex:
    • Bony and ligamentous ring consisting of the glenoid, coracoid, distal clavicle, acromion, CC ligaments, and AC joint
    • Injury to two or more components can cause shoulder instability and floating shoulder
  • Floating shoulder: fracture of the glenoid neck and the ipsilateral clavicle effectively dissociating the upper extremity from the axial skeleton
    • True floating shoulder is argued to require disruption to AC and CA ligaments or acromial fracture in addition to glenoid neck and clavicular fractures
  • Scapulothoracic dissociation: devastating injury involving lateralization of the scapula and upper extremity away from the thorax due to severe blunt force and typically includes injury to:
    • Upper extremity neurovascular structures (i.e. brachial plexus, subclavian or axillary arteries)
    • Muscles: rhomboids, levator scapulae, trapezius, latissimus dorsi, pectoralis
    • Clavicle, SC and/or AC joint
    • Can be identified by lateralization of the scapula on chest radiograph
    • Outcomes are generally poor: > 90% have neurologic injury, > 50% result in a flail upper extremity, 20% undergo amputation, 10% are fatal

  • Patients typically present with an adducted upper extremity with local tenderness and pain with abduction
  • Pain with deep inspiration and variable ecchymosis may be present
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
  • Scapular body fractures are associated with rib fractures, pneumo/hemothorax, pulmonary contusion, vertebral fractures, abdominal injury, head injury and long-bone fracture
  • Compartment syndrome is uncommon but must be ruled out in the presence of tense swelling of the supra/infraspinatus
    • Comolli sign: triangular swelling over scapula due to hematoma that can increase compartment pressure
