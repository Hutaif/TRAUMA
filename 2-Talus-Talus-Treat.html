Treatment
  • Objective is to minimize risk of osteonecrosis and reconstruct articular surfaces
  • Urgent reduction of dislocated and displaced fractures is warranted to preserve blood supply
  • With adequate reduction, operative treatment can be delayed to permit improvement of the soft tissues

    Hawkins I: no step off or incongruity on CT
  • SLC for 8-12 weeks
  • NWB for 8 weeks or until healing is demonstrated

    Hawkins II:
  • Immediate closed reduction or urgent open reduction if closed reduction is not possible
    • Flex knee to relax gastrocsoleus
    • Maximum plantarflexion and traction on foot brings the head in line with the body
    • Inversion/eversion of hindfoot reduces subtalar joint
    • Near anatomic reduction can reduce urgency for surgery
  • ORIF with interfragmentary compression screws ± plates (for comminution) is recommended post-reduction using one or a combination of the following approaches
    • Anteromedial: provides easy access to the neck but is less extensile and may disrupt blood supply
      • Preserve saphoneous vein, nerve and deltoid artery
      • Can be used for anterior to posterior screw placement through head or provisional fixation in combination with posterolateral approach
    • Anterolateral: allows placement of lateral screw through head
      • Preserve the artery to the sinus tarsi
      • Improves visualization of sinus tarsi, lateral neck and the subtalar joint
    • Posteror: allows posterior to anterior screw placement (superior mechanically to anterior to posterior) for talar neck fractures
  • Percutaneous fixation with lag screws can be considered for fractures that are satisfactorily closed reduced

    Hawkins III:
  • Dislocated talar bodies are rarely reducible closed and attempts to do so can increase complications (e.g. skin necrosis)
  • Urgent open reduction is warranted to relieve neurovascular compression, reduce risk for osteonecrosis and skin necrosis
  • ORIF per Hawkins II
  • Up to 50% are open fractures: require meticulous wound irrigation and debridement (infection rates up to 38%)

    Hawkins IV:
  • Urgent reduction is warranted as with Hawkins III
  • Risk of osteonecrosis of both the head and body is increased
  • Persistent instability of the talonavicular joint warrants pinning

    Talar body:
  • Involve the tibiotalar and subtalar (posterior facet) articulations, requiring open anatomic reduction
  • Anterolateral or anteromedial approaches are typically utilized (see Hawkins II above)
  • Medial or lateral malleolar osteotomy often required to place subarticular screws
  • Severe impaction may require bone grafting

    Lateral process: articulates with the subtalar and ankle joint (fibula)
  • Nonoperative: Small fragments or fragments with < 2 mm of displacement
    • NWB SLC for 4-6 weeks
  • Operative: large fragments, fragments with > 2 mm of displacement or fragments that involve the subtalar joint
    • ORIF with lag screws
    • Excision can be considered for small, comminuted fragments

    Talar head:
  • Usually due to impaction against navicular
  • Evaluate for navicular fracture and talonavicular subluxation
  • Nonoperative: indicated for minimally displaced fractures
    • NWB SLC for 6 weeks
    • Percutaneous fixation may facilitate immobilization
  • Operative: indications include displaced fractures or those with articular incongruity
    • Anterolateral or anteromedial approach to the talonavicular joint
    • Lag screws or buttress plating

    Posterior process (Shepard's fracture): involve the posterior talar articular surface
  • Usually occur with ankle inversion (avulsion of PTFL) or equinus (compression)
  • Nonoperative: indicated for minimally displaced fractures
    • SLC for 6 weeks, NWB for 4 weeks
  • Operative: indications include displaced fractures
    • Large fragments should undergo screw fixation or excision if small

Complications
  • Compartment syndrome: requires urgent fasciotomies to release pressure and avoid muscle death
  • Delayed union: defined as > 6 months and occurs in < 15%
  • Infection: open fractures require meticulous wound irrigation and debridement
  • Malunion: difficult to treat, prevention is important
    • Most commonly varus and dorsiflexion
    • May require osteotomy, subtalar or triple (subtalar, talonavicular and calcaneocuboid joints) arthrodesis to correct alignment
  • Nonunion: rare, defined as > 12 months
    • Can be treated with corticocancellous bone graft with rigid fixation to restore alignment
    • Symptomatic lateral process fragments can be excised
  • Osteonecrosis:
    • Chance of developing based on fracture type:
      • Head: 5-10%
      • Body: 38%
      • Hawkins I: 0-13%
      • Hawkins II: 20-50%
      • Hawkins III/IV: up to 100%
    • MRI as early as 3 weeks post-injury can identify osteonecrosis
    • Hawkins sign (subchondral osteopenia at 6-8 weeks) suggests viable bone and the absence of osteonecrosis
    • Pain can be eliminated with a tibiocalcaneal arthrodesis through remaining viable talar bone
  • Posttraumatic arthritis: a consequence of any articular injury, can be theoretically minimized with anatomic reduction
    • May occur in up to 40-90%, in either the tibiotalar, talonavicular or subtalar joints
    • May require arthrodesis or ankle arthroplasty
  • Skin slough: may be secondary to prolonged dislocation due to pressure necrosis of soft tissues
