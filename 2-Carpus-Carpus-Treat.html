Treatment
    Scaphoid:
  • A trial of immobilization for 1-2 weeks for clinical suspicion but negative radiographs
    • Short arm thumb spica splint or cast immobilization in the acute setting
    • Consider long arm spica cast for proximal pole, severely comminuted or unstable fractures
    • Repeat radiographs followed by CT if suspicion continues
    • Definitive treatment with a cast can take up to 12 weeks for proximal pole fractures
  • Nonoperative: indicated for nondisplaced fractures, particularly of the distal pole and waist
    • Long or short arm spica cast with wrist in neutral maintained for 4-8 weeks
      • Interval imaging is required to monitor for displacement
    • Healing rate:
      • Distal third: 100%
      • Waist: 80%
      • Proximal third: 60%
  • Operative: indications include displaced fractures (> 1 mm), unstable (Herbert type B) proximal pole fractures and nondisplaced waist fractures in high demand patients
    • Fixation achieved with headless variable-pitch compression screw (e.g. Herbert screw)
      • Volar approach lowers risk to vascular supply
      • Dorsal approach preferred for proximal pole fractures
    • Percutaneous screw placement can reduce risk of scarring, ligament and vascular injury
      • Primarily indicated for nondisplaced waist fractures
      • Displaced fractures can be reduced manually or with percutaneous K-wire joy sticks
      • Allows early ROM with removable splint

    Lunate
  • Dislocated or displaced fractures should generally undergo closed reduction
  • Nonoperative: indicated for nondisplaced fractures
    • Most lunate fractures heal well with a SAC for 4-6 weeks
    • Interval follow-up imaging and MRI are ideal to monitor healing
    • Fracture displacement increases risk for osteonecrosis (Kienböck's Disease)
  • Operative: indications include displaced (> 1 mm) or angulated fractures
    • Open reduction and screw fixation should be considered

    Triquetrum
  • Dislocated or displaced fractures should generally undergo closed reduction
  • Nonoperative: indicated for nondisplaced fractures
    • SAC or ulnar gutter splint for 4-6 weeks
  • Operative: indicated for displaced fractures
    • Open reduction and screw fixation

    Pisiform
  • Dislocated or displaced fractures should generally undergo closed reduction
  • Nonoperative: most fractures heal with conservative treatment
    • SAC or ulnar gutter splint for 4-6 weeks
  • Operative: indicated for persistent pain
    • Excision of the pisiform may be necessary if screw fixation cannot be performed

    Trapezium
  • Dislocated or displaced fractures should generally undergo closed reduction
  • Nonoperative: indicated for nondisplaced fractures
    • Thumb spica split or cast for 4-6 weeks
  • Operative: indications include articular fractures, comminuted fractures and displaced fractures
    • Open reduction and screw fixation
    • Fusion or arthroplasty may be required for severe comminution

    Trapezoid
  • Dislocated or displaced fractures should generally undergo closed reduction
  • Nonoperative: indicated for nondisplaced fractures
    • SAC or splint for 4-6 weeks
  • Operative: indications include displaced and articular fractures
    • Open reduction and screw fixation

    Capitate
  • Dislocated or displaced fractures should generally undergo closed reduction
  • Nonoperative: indicated for nondisplaced fractures
    • SAC for 4-6 weeks
  • Operative: indicated for displaced fractures
    • Open reduction and screw fixation

    Hamate
  • Dislocated or displaced fractures should generally undergo closed reduction
  • Nonoperative: indicated for nondisplaced fractures
    • SAC or splint for 4-6 weeks
  • Operative: indicated for displaced fractures
    • Excision of a hook fragment may be required for nonunion or entrapment of the ulnar nerve

Complications
    Scaphoid
  • Malunion
  • Nonunion (5-25%): leads to wrist arthritis
    • Occurs more frequently with SAC, displacement > 1 mm, unstable, proximal fracture, osteonecrosis and smoking
    • Scaphoid nonunion advanced collapse (SNAC): degenerative changes in the radioscaphoid joint, followed by lunocapitate joint
    • Treat with autogenous bone grafting and screw fixation
  • Preiser's Disease: osteonecrosis of the scaphoid
    • Occurs most commonly with proximal pole fractures
    • Minimize with early immobilization and early open reduction if needed

    Lunate
  • Nonunion: Can be treated with autogenous bone grafting and screw fixation
  • Osteonecrosis: Kienböck's disease may require salvage procedures such as proximal row carpectomy or arthodesis

    Triquetrum
  • Posttraumatic arthritis
  • Ulnar carpal instability: due to abnormal mechanics resulting from non/malunion

    Pisiform
  • Calcific tendinitis
  • Posttraumatic arthritis
  • Ulnar neuropathy: due to subluxation of the pisiform

    Trapezium
  • Posttraumatic arthritis: may require fusion or arthroplasty

    Trapezoid
  • Posttraumatic arthritis: may require fusion or arthroplasty

    Capitate
  • Osteonecrosis: single vessel blood supply, may require autogenous bone grafting
  • Posttraumatic arthritis

    Hamate
  • 5th digit flexor tendon rupture
  • Symptomatic nonunion: can be treated with excision of the hook fragment
  • Ulnar neuropathy: due to fracture displacement
