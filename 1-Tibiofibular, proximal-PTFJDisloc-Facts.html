Facts
  • Rare injury that is classically associated with horseback riding where violent knee twisting can occur (e.g. foot stuck in stirrup while falling off horse)
  • Proximal tibiofibular joint (PTFJ) dislocations are rarely an isolated injury, usually occur as part of other lower extremity injuries (e.g. posterior hip dislocations)
  • Dislocations usually occur with knee flexed

  • Located along the posterolateral surface of the proximal tibia
  • The common peroneal nerve courses around the proximal fibular neck approximately 3 cm inferior to the knee joint line
  • Inherently stable joint: fibular head is seated in a groove with strong ligamentous support
    • Joint can have a horizontal or oblique orientation
      • Oblique: defined as > 20° of inclination to horizontal, is more constrained and thus is at higher risk of dislocation
    • Joint capsule: anterior is thicker than posterior
    • Posterior tibiofibular ligament: extends from fibular head to posterior tibial condyle
    • LCL: provides additional support in knee extension
    • Popliteus: reinforces posterior tibiofibular ligament
  • Bears approximately 1/6 of the weight in the leg

  • Patient presents with lateral knee pain
  • High-energy mechanisms require a full trauma evaluation and resuscitation
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
  • Patients typically demonstrate tenderness to palpation over the fibular head
  • Topography of lateral knee may be visibly different from contralateral, uninjured side and comparison of landmarks may be useful in diagnosis
