Treatment
  • Greater tuberosity fracture: more common with anterior dislocations
    • Superior or posterior displacement may result in subacromial impingement
    • Displacement > 5 mm warrants fixation via a lateral deltoid splitting approach
  • Fractures of the greater or lesser tuberosities may reduce after reduction of dislocation and can be treated nonoperatively

  • Initial management: closed reduction under conscious sedation or intraarticular lidocaine
    • Fractures of the humeral neck preclude closed reduction

    Anterior:
  • Closed reduction techniques:
    • Hippocratic: foot in axilla, gentle traction with IR/ER movements
    • Stimson: prone, arm hanging off stretcher
      • Gentle (5-10 lbs) traction is applied for 15-20 minutes
      • Spontaneous reduction occurs due to muscle relaxation
      • Addition of elbow flexion can facilitate relaxation of the biceps
    • Milch: supine, arm abducted followed by ER, thumb pressure anteriorly over the humeral head induces reduction
    • Kocher: arm adducted, elbow flexed 90°, traction along humerus, ER of shoulder to unlock proximal humerus followed by forward flexion of arm in sagittal plane and IR across the body
      • Fallen out of favor due to complications including glenoid fracture
    • Keep arm adducted and immobilized with a sling and swathe for 2-5 weeks

  • Operative: indications include open dislocation, irreducible dislocation, fracture-dislocation of the humeral neck, anterior glenoid involving > 25% of the anterior articular surface or with > 5 mm of step-off, displaced tuberosity fracture
    • Large glenoid fractures require screw fixation (see Scapula Fracture chapter)
    • Displaced tuberosity fractures post-reduction can be treated with suture of screw fixation (see Proximal Humerus Fracture chapter)
    • Fracture-dislocations involving the humeral neck require ORIF (see Proximal Humerus Fracture chapter)
    • Glenohumeral stabilization options include labral repair (Bankart), capsulorraphy, and/or transfers of muscle, tendon or bone
      • Putti-Platt (subscapularis plication) is one common technique to correct instability by reducing external rotation
      • Latarjet: coracoid transfer to the glenoid, acting as a buttress and extending the glenoid surface

    Posterior:
  • Closed reduction techniques:
    • Traction to adducted arm with gentle lifting of proximal humerus into glenoid
    • Do not externally rotate (may fracture proximal humerus)
    • Consider lateral traction to unlock humeral head
    • Sling and swathe for 3-6 weeks
      • Better stability may be achieved with ER if there is a large anteromedial defect

  • Operative: indications include displaced tuberosity fracture, posterior glenoid fragment involving > 33% of the posterior articular surface or with > 5 mm of step-off, irreducible dislocation, open dislocation, fracture-dislocation of the humeral neck, reverse Hill-Sachs lesion
    • Reverse Hill-Sachs: large lesions can cause recurrent dislocations with internal rotation
      • Lesions involving < 20% of articular surface can be treated with lesser tuberosity/subscapularis transfer (McLaughlin)
      • Involving < 40%: with osteochondral allograft or bone grafting
      • Involving > 40%: with hemiarthroplasty
    • Displaced tuberosity fractures post-reduction can be treated with suture of screw fixation (see Proximal Humerus Fracture chapter)
    • Large glenoid fractures require screw fixation (see Scapula Fracture chapter)
    • Fracture-dislocations involving the humeral neck require ORIF (see Proximal Humerus Fracture chapter)
    • Glenohumeral stabilization options include reverse Putti-Platt (plication of the infraspinatus and teres minor), Boyd-Sisk (biceps tendon transfer to posterior glenoid) and capsulorraphy

    Inferior:
  • Closed reduction techniques:
    • Axial traction in line with humerus with gradual decrease in shoulder abduction
    • Counter-traction by assistant on the thorax and shoulder
    • Sling and swathe post-reduction

  • Operative:
    • Closed reduction may not be achievable when the proximal humerus button-holes through the inferior capsule
    • Open reduction and repair of the soft tissues is required in these cases

    Superior:
  • Closed reduction techniques:
    • Axial and lateral traction with counter-traction by assistant in the axilla
    • Sling and swathe post-reduction

  • Operative:
    • Open reduction and repair of the soft tissues is required when closed reduction is not achievable

Complications
  • Glenohumeral instability: young patients with traumatic dislocations have the greatest risk of recurrent dislocation
    • MRI followed by early arthroscopic examination and repair of injured structures may reduce long-term morbidity
  • Neurologic injury: axillary nerve is most common but brachial plexus may be involved
    • Electrodiagnostic testing may be warranted
    • Recovery typically occurs over 3-6 months
    • Exploration should be considered if improvement does not occur by 2-3 months
  • Osteonecrosis: more common with fracture-dislocation involving the anatomic neck
    • Risk increases with displacement
  • Posttraumatic arthritis
  • Shoulder stiffness: may respond to manipulation and physical therapy initially
    • Higher risk after arthroplasty for fracture-dislocation
