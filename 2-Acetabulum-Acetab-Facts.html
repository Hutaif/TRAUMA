Facts
  • Latin for a small cup used to store vinegar
  • Fractures occur primarily in young adults, resulting from high-energy mechanisms such as MVC and high velocity falls
  • Geriatric acetabular fractures are typically low-energy falls

  • Acetabulum is formed by the confluence of the ilium, ischium and pubis within the innominate bone
  • Quadrilateral plate forms the medial wall
  • 40% of femoral head is covered by the acetabulum
  • Hip joint has inherent stability due to bony and soft tissue anatomy
    • Fibrocartilanginous labrum deepens the acetabulum, increasing coverage and stability
    • Thick capsule and ligaments (iliofemoral, pubofemoral and ischiofemoral) provide further support
    • Soft tissue structures including the labrum, iliofemoral ligament, capsule, bony fragments and tendons (piriformis, psoas) can prevent closed reduction
  • Fracture pattern determined by position of the femoral head and direction of applied force
  • Two column concept:
    • Anterior column (iliopubic): extends from the iliac crest to the pubic symphysis and includes the anterior half of the acetabulum
    • Posterior column (ilioischial): extends from the greater sciatic notch to the ischial tuberosity and includes the posterior half of the acetabulum
    • Acetabulum often described as an inverted Y
      • Anterior column/iliopubic segment forms anterior limb
      • Posterior column/ilioischial segment forms posterior limb
      • The arch between the limbs forms the roof of the acetabulum

  • Patients typically present after high-energy trauma
  • A full trauma resuscitation and evaluation should be performed
    • Up to 50% are associated with other injuries including cranial, chest and abdominal
    • Life threatening injuries are first priority
  • Soft tissues warrant close inspection for abrasions and local wounds
    • Morel-Lavallée lesion: internal degloving injury leading to a serosanguinous fluid filled cavity
      • Affected area (usually over the greater trochanter) demonstrates swelling, fluctuance and ecchymosis
      • Associated with a high rate of infection (up to 33%)
      • Requires aggressive debridement and drainage
  • Position of the affected extremity can suggest anterior dislocation (abduction, ER), posterior dislocation (adduction, flexion, IR) or other ipsilateral fractures (shortening)
  • Assess NV status of the pelvis and the lower extremities
    • Sciatic nerve injuries occur in up to 20% of posterior column or wall fractures
      • Peroneal division is more commonly affected than tibial (test tibialis anterior, EHL and peroneals)
  • Other musculoskeletal injuries are common
    • Dashboard injuries typically result in posterior wall fractures
      • Note injuries to knee including joint instability and patellar fractures
    • Pelvic fractures are present in up to 20% of cases
    • Presence of femoral shaft fractures can affect operative management
