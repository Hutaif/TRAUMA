Treatment
    Scapulothoracic dissociation:
  • The result of severe trauma, requires full trauma evaluation and resuscitation
  • Following resuscitation, emergent management is based on vascular injury: obtain angiogram to locate injury followed by vascular repair
  • Brachial plexus should be explored at the time of vascular repair
  • Stabilization of associated musculoskeletal injuries protects vascular repair
  • Based on injuries, above elbow amputation with shoulder fusion may be considered

    Glenoid neck: anatomic reduction is not crucial
  • Nonoperative: most common, indicated for type I medial fractures if the clavicle, coracoid and CC ligament are intact
    • Sling immobilization of the shoulder for comfort with early passive ROM exercises
  • Operative: indications include lateral fractures, type II medial fractures, GPA < 20° and concomittant clavicle fracture (floating shoulder)
    • Performed through a posterior approach using reconstruction or mini-fragment plates
  • Floating shoulder:
    • Outcomes of surgical and nonsurgical treatment are similar when fractures independently are nonoperative
    • Plate fixation of a clavicle fracture may allow better pain control and faster rehabilitation
    • Ligamentotaxis can be used to indirectly reduce the glenoid fragment when reducing or fixing other shoulder fractures (i.e. clavicular or scapular spine fracture)

    Glenoid fossa: often occurs with shoulder dislocation
  • Reduce shoulder dislocation if present (see Shoulder Dislocation chapter)
  • Nonoperative: indicated for minimally displaced fractures with concentric glenohumeral reduction
    • Sling and swathe followed by early ROM and close radiographic follow up
  • Operative: indications include fracture displacement leading to humeral head subluxation, fractures with step-off > 5 mm at the articular surface and type IA fractures involving > 25% of the articular surface or type IB fractures involving > 33% articular surface
    • Performed through deltapectoral or posterior approach based on fracture location
    • Reconstruction can be achieved with screw fixation

    Acromion:
  • Nonoperative: indicated for nondisplaced fractures
    • Sling immobilization for 3 weeks
  • Operative: indications include displaced fractures
    • Requires open reduction and fixation with tension band construct, screws or plating
    • Fractures at the base of the acromion are at high risk for nonunion

    Body fractures:
  • Generally nonoperative: symptomatic sling immobilization followed by early shoulder motion
  • Open reduction may be considered when exploration is necessary for neurovascular compromise

    Coracoid:
  • Generally nonoperative: symptomatic sling immobilization followed by early shoulder motion
  • Fractures associated with type 3 AC joint separation should be fixed

Complications
  • Malunion: generally well tolerated except when subacromial space is encroached or when glenohumeral stability is compromised
  • Shoulder weakness
  • Shoulder stiffness
  • Suprascapular nerve injury: associated with body, neck or coracoid fractures that involve the suprascapular notch
  • Symptomatic nonunion
