Treatment
  • Cleanse and dress open wounds acutely
  • Apply long leg posterior splint in anticipation of definitive care

  • Nonoperative: indicated for minimally displaced (< 2 mm), stable fractures, particularly in elderly patients or those with significant comorbid medical conditions that preclude operative treatment
    • Minimally displaced fractures can be treated with a NWB LLC with the knee flexed to 20° for 4-8 weeks
    • Early mobilization can be achieved with hinged knee brace
    • Higher energy fractures can be treated in skeletal traction with ROM exercises, followed by cast immobilization

  • Operative: indications include displaced (> 2 mm), unstable or open fractures, compartment syndrome and vascular injuries
    • Objective is to reconstruct the articular surface, recreate normal tibial alignment and provide soft tissue coverage over the fracture
    • Arthroscopy can be used to evaluate the articular surface and assist in fracture reduction
    • External fixation can be used if the patient is unfit for surgery or has compromised soft tissues
    • Consider delaying surgery until swelling has subsided and local skin conditions have improved
    • Fracture can be approached through a anterolateral or posteromedial incision depending on location

    Type I-III: low-energy
    • Reduce joint surface under direct visualization
    • Fill voids left by elevation of depressed articular fragments with bone graft
    • Provisional fixation with K-wires
    • "Rafting" 3.5 mm screws placed subchondrally provide support for articular surface
    • Precontoured lateral plates provide buttress

    Type IV-VI: high-energy
    • Temporary knee-spanning external fixation allows restoration of alignment in the setting of soft tissue swelling, open fracture or other need to delay surgery
      • Allows for ongoing wound care and faster resolution of soft tissue swelling
    • Knee-sparing ring or hybrid fixator can be used as definitive fixation in the setting of severe soft tissue injury
      • Condylar fractures can be stabilized by lag screws
      • Wires capture the articular block allowing fixation to the metaphysis via the fixator
    • Open reduction can be achieved via posteromedial ± anterolateral incisions
    • Rafting screws can be placed in combination with pre-contoured buttress plates
    • Less Invasive Stabilization System (LISS): particularly useful for metaphyseal comminution
      • Acts as a laterally based internal fixator with locking fixed angle screws
      • Preserves periosteal blood supply by not depending on bone-plate friction
      • After articular injury is addressed, this precontoured plate is passed submuscularly over proximal tibia in antegrade fashion, bridging any metaphyseal comminution
      • Utilizes indirect fracture reduction and limited surgical dissection
      • Screws are placed percutaneously using special jigs

  • Fasciotomy:
    • Compartment pressure > 30 mmHg or within 30 mmHg of the diastolic blood pressure warrants urgent fasciotomies of the leg compartments (anterior, lateral, deep/superficial posterior)
    • Can be performed through two incisions
      • Anterolateral allows release of the anterior and lateral compartments
      • Medial incision allows release of the superficial and deep posterior compartments

Complications
  • Compartment syndrome: rare but increased risk with higher energy closed injuries
    • Urgent fasciotomy is required to release pressure and avoid muscle death
  • Infection: 10% of high-energy fractures
    • Can be due to ill-timed incisions through compromised skin
    • May require delayed primary closure or flap coverage
  • Knee stiffness: common and can be related to initial injury, surgical scarring or prolonged immobilization
  • Loss of fixation: related to bone quality
    • Often results in varus deformity
  • Malunion/nonunion: most commonly Schatzker VI at the metaphyseal-diaphyseal junction
    • Related to comminution, unstable fixation, infection and implant failure
    • Malunions of > 10° are correlated with poorer outcomes
  • Peroneal nerve injury: most common with lateral injuries
  • Popliteal artery injuries: increased risk with tibial plateau fractures associated with knee dislocation
  • Posttraumatic arthritis: a consequence of any articular injury, can be theoretically minimized with anatomic reduction
  • Thromboembolic disease: present in up to 10% of cases
