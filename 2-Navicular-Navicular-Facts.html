Facts
  • Isolated injuries are rare
  • Usually the result of a direct blow or loading along the axis of the foot

  • Articulates with the three cuneiforms distally, the cuboid laterally and the talus proximally
    • Strong ligaments anchor the navicular to the cuneiforms
    • Also serves as the insertion of the calcaneonavicular (spring), tibionavicular portion of the superficial deltoid and talonavicular ligaments
  • Two column concept: structural description of the foot
    • Medial: 1st, 2nd and 3rd MTs, the 3 cuneiforms, navicular and talus
    • Lateral: 4th and 5th MTs, cuboid and calcaneus
  • Accessory navicular (os tibiale externum) is present in 25% and bilateral in 64% of cases
    • Located within the posterior tibial tendon which attaches medially
  • Blood supply from the posterior tibial (medial), medial plantar artery (plantar) and dorsalis pedis (dorsal) arteries
    • Medial and lateral areas are well vascularized compared to central portion where stress fractures often occur (watershed area)
  • Stress fractures: commonly associated with running activities
    • Fracture line usually sagittally oriented
    • Usually nondisplaced

  • Patients present with pain along the dorsomedial arch and variable amounts of swelling and ecchymosis
  • Tenderness to palpation can usually be elicited over the navicular
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
  • Navicular fractures are often associated with injuries to adjacent structures (e.g. cuboid)
