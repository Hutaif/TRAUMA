Facts
  • Common injury of young males (middle third) and the elderly (lateral/medial third)
  • Generally due to a direct blow to the shoulder
  • Middle/lateral third junction (~75%): thin and without muscular attachment making it vulnerable to fracture
  • Lateral fractures (~25%)
  • Medial fractures (~2%)

  • First bone to ossify (5th week of gestation) and last ossification center to fuse (25 years)
  • Only bone to ossify by intramembranous ossification without endochondral ossification
  • Lateral third is flat, medial third is tubular
  • Acts as a strut, connecting the upper extremity to the thorax
  • Articulates with the acromion laterally: acromioclavicular (AC) and coracoclavicular (CC) ligaments stabilize this articulation
    • CC ligament has two components: trapezoid (lateral) and conoid (medial)
  • Articulates with the sternum medially: sternoclavicular (SC) joint capsule, costoclavicular ligaments, subclavius muscle and the interclavicular ligament stabilize this articulation
  • Brachial plexus and subclavian artery/vein are at risk for injury with displaced fractures
  • Anterior to the clavicle lie the supraclavicular nerves that innervate the skin of the lower neck and upper chest
    • At risk for injury during surgical approach to mid-clavicle

  • Patients present with pain and variable swelling and deformity
  • Tenderness to palpation is common
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
    • High-energy injuries are associated with pneumo/hemothorax, lung contusion, rib fracture, upper extremity injury and head injury
    • Hematoma, local bruit, absent pulses, paresthesias and/or distal weakness should raise suspicion of subclavian or brachial plexus injury and warrants further investigation
  • Condition of the overlying skin and any open wounds should be noted
    • Pressure on the skin from bone displacement can cause necrosis and/or convert to open fracture
