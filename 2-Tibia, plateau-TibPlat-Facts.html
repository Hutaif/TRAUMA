Facts
  • Diverse group of fractures that result from axial loading with valgus (more common) or varus stress
    • 70-80% of fractures involve lateral plateau
    • 10-20% involve the medial plateau
    • 10-30% are bicondylar
  • Highest incidence in the fifth decade of life
  • Eldery patients are more likely to have depression fractures due to osteopenic bone
  • Younger patients are more likely to have ligamentous disruption

  • Lateral plateau is convex and sits slightly above the medial plateau, creating 3° of varus
    • Overall knee alignment is 7° of valgus due to geometry of femur
  • Medial plateau is concave, larger and stronger than the lateral plateau
  • Tibial plateau has 6-10° of posterior slope
  • Both medial and lateral tibial condyles have fibrocartilaginous meniscii that serve to increase the contact area with the distal femur and absorb shock
  • Tibial spines (intercondylar eminences) are nonarticular
    • Serve as attachment points for the meniscii
    • ACL inserts anterior and between the two spines
    • PCL inserts along the posterior proximal tibia
  • MCL inserts broadly along the medial and posteriomedial tibia
  • LCL inserts on the fibular head
  • Important landmarks include the tibial tubercle anteriorly (patellar tendon insertion), the pes anserinus medially (means goose foot in Latin, insertion of the medial hamstring - sartorius, gracilis, semitendinosis) and Gerdy's tubercle laterally (iliotibial band insertion)
  • Anteromedial tibia is subcutaneous
    • At risk for open wounds
  • Neurovascular structures at risk include the popliteal artery and peroneal nerve
    • Popliteal vessels emerge from adductor hiatus to enter the popliteal fossa posteriorly
      • Five geniculate branches
      • Proceeds under soleus through a fibrous tunnel
      • Trifurcation into posterior tibial, peroneal and anterior tibial arteries occur near this point
    • Common peroneal nerve courses around the fibular neck

  • Patients typically present with knee pain and variable degrees of swelling and ecchymosis
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries including compartment syndrome and vascular injuries
    • Medial plateau fractures are at higher risk of neurovascular injury due to higher energy
    • Vascular disruption are more commonly intimal tears or thrombosis
    • Meniscal tears occur in 50%
    • Ligamentous disruption, including cruciates, is present in up to 30%
      • Can be examined under anesthesia after fracture fixation has been achieved
      • Joint widening of more than 10° with varus/valgus testing suggests ligamentous injury
  • Open fractures must be ruled out in the presence of any skin lacerations
    • A saline injection into the joint can be used to determine continuity with lacerations
    • Perform arthrocentesis (remove hemarthrosis) followed by injection of saline (~50 cc)
    • Extravasation from the wound indicates continuity with the joint and open fracture
    • Presence of an open fracture or joint involvement requires urgent surgical debridement
  • Compartment syndrome: sustained increase in compartment pressure due to inflammation and edema in one or more of the leg compartments leading to vascular compromise and muscle death
    • Muscle death occurs 6-8 hours after the onset of compartment syndrome
    • Severe pain with passive motion of the toes/foot is often first sign
    • Other signs include tense swelling of the calf, diminished pulses, pain out of proportion to injury and paresthesias
      • Serial neurovascular exams are required as compartment syndrome can develop more than 24 hours after injury
    • Compartment pressures should be measured in patients with clincal signs of compartment syndrome
      • Compartment pressure > 30 mmHg or within 30 mmHg of the diastolic blood pressure warrant urgent fasciotomies of the leg compartments
    • Deep posterior compartment pressure may be elevated in the presence of a soft superficial compartment
      • Missed posterior compartment syndrome can result in claw toe deformity
