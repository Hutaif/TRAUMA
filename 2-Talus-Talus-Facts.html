Facts
  • Fractures of the neck are most common (> 50%)
    • Typically result from hyperdorsiflexion injuries where talar neck impacts on distal anterior tibia (e.g. MVCs and falls from height)
  • Body fractures occur with axial loading between the tibia and calcaneus

  • Approximately 60% of the talus is covered in cartilage
  • Talus has 5 anatomic regions:
    • Body: articulates superiorly with the tibial plafond, medially and laterally with the malleoli, inferiorly with the posterior facet of the calcaneus
      • Anterior aspect of the dome is wider than posterior
    • Head: articulates anteriorly with the navicular and inferiorly with the sustentaculum tali of the calcaneus
    • Neck: region between the body and head
    • Lateral process: articulates with the posterior facet of the calcaneus and lateral malleolus
    • Posterior process: between medial and lateral tubercles of this process is a groove for the flexor hallucis longus (FHL)
      • Os trigonum is present in 50%: represents a separate ossification center posterior to the posterior process and can be confused for a fracture
  • No muscles originate or insert on the talus
  • Tarsal canal: tunnel created by the coaptation of the talar sulcus and the interosseous sulcus of the calcaneus (between the middle and posterior facets)
    • Creates a tunnel that runs transversely between the talus and calcaneus
    • Opens up into the tarsal sinus laterally
  • Vascular supply: predisposed to osteonecrosis due to limited vascular perforation
    • Blood supply to body enters at the neck and proceeds posteriorly
      • Anastomotic sling along inferior neck formed by artery of the sinus tarsi and the artery of the tarsal canal
      • Fractures of the neck disrupt this vascular sling
    • Artery of the sinus tarsi: derived from branches of the peroneal and dorsalis pedis
    • Artery of the tarsal canal (posterior tibial): primary blood supply to body
    • Deltoid artery (posterior tibial): supplies medial third of the body
      • May be only remaining supply in displaced fractures of the neck or body
    • Dorsal neck branches (dorsalis pedis): supplies a portion of the head
    • Posterior process vessels (peroneal): make minor contributions to the posterior process
    • Most of the blood supply to the head and neck are from the dorsalis pedis
    • Displaced fractures of the talar neck disrupt the dorsal neck branches, the artery to the sinus tarsi and the artery to the tarsal canal
  • Spring (calcaneonavicular) ligament: inferior to talar head and acts like a sling to suspend
  • Deltoid ligament (tibiotalar portion) attaches at the medial talar body
  • Foot compartments:
    • Medial: abductor hallucis, flexor hallucis brevis
    • Interosseous: 7 interossei muscles
    • Central superficial: flexor digitorum brevis, adductor hallucis, lumbricals
    • Central deep (calcaneal): quadratus plantae
    • Lateral: flexor digiti minimi brevis, abductor digiti minimi, opponens digiti minimi

  • Patients present with pain with weight-bearing and ROM and demonstrate variable swelling and ecchymosis
  • High-energy injuries require a full trauma evaluation and resuscitation
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
    • High-energy injuries are often associated with injury to the soft tissue envelope
      • Up to 20% of talar fractures are open
    • Concomitant malleolar fractures are common with talar body fractures
    • Calcaneal fractures are assocatied with talar neck fractures
  • Lateral process fractures have tenderness 1 cm inferior to the tip of the lateral malleolus
    • Due to inversion (avulsion) or inversion (impaction)
    • Classically associated with snowboarding
    • Often mistaken for ankle sprain
  • Posterior process fractures (Shepard's fractures) may be demonstrate FHL irritation
  • Tenderness near the talonavicular joint medially may be elicited in talar head fractures
  • Compartment syndrome: sustained increase in compartment pressure due to inflammation and edema in one or more of the foot compartments leading to vascular compromise and muscle death
    • Crush injuries are particularly susceptible
    • Signs include diminished pulses, severe swelling and pain out of proportion to injury, particularly with passive extension of the digits
    • Compartment pressures should be measured when signs of compartment syndrome are present
    • Urgent fasciotomy of the compartments is warranted for pressures within 30 mmHg of the diastolic blood pressure
