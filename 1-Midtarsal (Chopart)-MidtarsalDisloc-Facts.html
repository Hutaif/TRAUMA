Facts
  • Injuries usually due to high-energy trauma (e.g. MVC)

  • The midtarsal joint (also known as the transverse tarsal or Chopart joint) consists of the calcaneocuboid and talonavicular joints
    • Acts in concert with subtalar joint in inversion/eversion
    • More mobile with heel in pronation
  • Two column concept: structural description of the foot
    • Medial: 1st, 2nd and 3rd MTs, the 3 cuneiforms, navicular and talus
    • Lateral: 4th and 5th MTs, cuboid and calcaneus
  • Cuboid acts as link between three naviculocuneiform joints
  • Talonavicular joint motion should be preserved when possible due to importance in kinematics
  • Ligamentous attachments include:
    • Calcaneonavicular (spring) ligament: strong plantar complex, primary restraint for talonavicular joint
    • Calcaeneocuboid and cuboideonavicular ligaments: support transverse tarsal joint
  • Acetabulum pedis: a deep socket that holds the head of the talus
    • Consists of navicular, anterior and middle calcaneal facets, calcaneonavicular and bifurcate ligaments
  • Foot compartments:
    • Medial: abductor hallucis, flexor hallucis brevis
    • Interosseous: 7 interossei muscles
    • Central superficial: flexor digitorum brevis, adductor hallucis, lumbricals
    • Central deep (calcaneal): quadratus plantae
    • Lateral: flexor digiti minimi brevis, abductor digiti minimi, opponens digiti minimi

  • Patients usually present with tenderness dorsally and deformity
  • High-energy mechanisms warrant a full trauma evaluation and resuscitation
    • Stress maneuvers to the midfoot may reproduce pain
  • Injuries may be bony, ligamentous or combined
  • Joint dislocations often reduce spontaneously prior to presentation
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
  • Compartment syndrome: sustained increase in compartment pressure due to inflammation and edema in one or more of the foot compartments leading to vascular compromise and muscle death
    • Crush injuries are particularly susceptible
    • Signs include diminished pulses, severe swelling and pain out of proportion to injury, particularly with passive extension of the digits
    • Compartment pressures should be measured when signs of compartment syndrome are present
    • Urgent fasciotomy of the compartments is warranted for pressures within 30 mmHg of the diastolic blood pressure
