Treatment
  • Damage control orthopedics: in polytrauma patients, initial interventions should provide expedient stabilization of long bone and pelvic fractures (e.g. external fixation)
    • Definitive procedures should be delayed until the patient's general condition has stabilized
    • Minimizes the "second hit" associated with physiological burden of surgery in patients at risk of multi-organ failure and acute respiratory distress
    • Patients at risk typically demonstrate hemodynamic instability, difficult resuscitation, polytrauma, coagulopathy and hypothermia
    • Immunological markers such as IL-6 can be used to monitor inflammatory response of at risk patients

  • Acceptable alignment:
    • < 5° of varus/valgus
    • < 5°-10° of anterior/posterior angulation
    • < 5° of rotational deformity (ER is better tolerated)
    • < 1 cm shortening (5 mm of distraction can delay healing by 8-12 months)
    • > 50% cortical contact

  • Nonoperative: indicated for minimal soft tissue injury (low-energy mechanisms) and fractures in acceptable alignment
    • Nonoperative treatment associated with lower rate of infection but operative treatment associated with higher rate of union at 20 weeks
    • Reduction can be performed with general anesthesia or conscious sedation
    • Initially place LLC with 30° of knee flexion to prevent weight-bearing, ankle at 90°
    • Weight-bearing can be advanced based on stability/comminution of fracture, degree of healing
    • Cast can then be replaced with knee at 0°-5° to allow for weight-bearing
    • Patellar tendon-bearing cast or functional brace can be applied after swelling has subsided
    • Total immobilization time depends on status of healing/union

  • Operative: indications include high-energy fracture, unacceptable alignment, compartment syndrome, open fracture, ipsilateral femoral fracture or intact fibula (relative)
    • Acutely cleanse and dress any open wounds in anticipation of thorough open irrigation and debridement in the OR
    • Apply long leg posterior splint acutely to minimize further soft tissue injury in anticipation of surgery

    External fixation: primarily used to treat open fractures, fractures complicated by compartment syndrome and patients with canal diameters < 6 mm (higher rate of intramedullary nail failure)
    • Allows for continuing wound care
    • 10% incidence of pin tract infections (most common complication)
    • Union rates of 85-95% with definitive external fixation
    • Can be transitioned to definitive IM nail within 2 weeks
    • Increased risk of infection when transition to definitive intramedullary nail is performed > 2 weeks after placement of external fixator

    Plate fixation: indications include patients with canal diameters < 6 mm, knee replacements and fractures extending into the metaphysis or epiphysis
    • Historically high risk of wound complications
    • Led to recent development of percutaneous techniques
    • Locking plates provide better fixation in osteoporotic bone
    • May be applied along medial (subcutaneous) or lateral (submuscular) tibia

    Intramedullary nailing: generally preferred for most fractures
    • Allows preservation of periosteal blood supply, minimizes soft tissue damage, provides control of alignment and permits weight-bearing
    • Contraindications: gross canal contamination, canal deformity, knee replacement or arthrodesis, canal diameters < 6 mm
    • Locked: provides rotational control and prevents shortening in presence of comminution
      • Locking screws can be removed later to dynamize and encourage healing
    • Unlocked: allows impaction of the fracture with weight-bearing for select transverse and short oblique fracture patterns
    • Reaming: generally believed to shorten time to union and lower incidence of nonunion
      • Allows uniform enlargement of the intramedullary canal
      • Advantage: allows for proper implant sizing, produces bone graft that may augment fracture healing, allows insertion of larger nail and thus more rigid fixation
      • Disadvantage: disrupts endosteal blood supply, increased risk for fat embolism
    • Unreamed: preserves the endosteal blood supply in open fractures where the periosteal supply has been disrupted
    • While reaming has a demonstrated advantage in clinical outcomes for closed tibial shaft fractures, this is not true for open fractures

  • Tibial stess fracture:
    • Activity modification or SLC and PWB for 6-10 weeks if necessary

  • Fibular fracture:
    • WBAT without immobilization or LLC based on symptoms
    • Nonunion is uncommon due to extensive muscular attachments

  • Fasciotomy:
    • Compartment pressure > 30 mmHg or within 30 mmHg of the diastolic blood pressure warrants urgent fasciotomies of the leg compartments (anterior, lateral, deep/superficial posterior)
    • Can be performed through two incisions
      • Anterolateral allows release of the anterior and lateral compartments
      • Medial incision allows release of the superficial and deep posterior compartments

Complications
  • Compartment syndrome (8%): lower age is an independent risk factor
    • Urgent fasciotomy is indicated to release compartment pressure and maintain muscle viability
  • Delayed union: defined as absence of healing at 20 weeks
    • Can be treated with bone grafting, dynamization of a locked nail (remove distal screws to allow fracture compression with weight-bearing) or exchange nailing with a larger nail
  • Hardware failure: screw breakage after nail placement is associated with unreamed nails (smaller diameter thus smaller interlocks)
  • Infection: < 5% of closed fractures
    • Generally associated with plate fixation, grade II and III open fractures
    • Infections involving the fracture site will lead to nonunion
  • Knee/ankle stiffness: due to prolonged immobilization (ankle more susceptible)
  • Knee pain: most common complication following IM nailing, occuring in > 50%
    • Unclear etiology but can be due to nail prominence
    • Resolves in 50% after nail removal
  • Malunion: associated with nonoperative treatment, external fixation, intact fibula and intramedullary nail treatment of proximal third tibia fractures
  • Neurovascular injury:
    • Anterior tibial artery is most at risk as it passes through the interosseous membrane
    • Popliteal artery can be injured during placement of a proximal anteroposterior IM nail interlock
    • Common peroneal nerve as it curves around fibular neck
    • Deep peroneal nerve is at risk during placement of external fixator pins or proximal IM nail interlocks
    • Sural and saphenous nerves are at risk during placement of distal IM nail interlocks
  • Nonunion: associated with open fractures (grade III), infection, nonoperative treatment and external fixation
    • Nonunion can be identified radiographically at 36 weeks when bone ends become sclerotic in the absence of bridging bone
    • Can be treated with cancellous bone graft exchange nailing with a larger nail
