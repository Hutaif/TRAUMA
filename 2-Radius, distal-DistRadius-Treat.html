Treatment
  • Closed reduction:
    • Objective: restore acceptable alignment
    • Perform neurovascular evaluation pre- and post-reduction
    • Provide anesthesia via hematoma block
      • Plain lidocaine is injected directly into fracture site
      • Can be effectively applied within 24-48 hours after injury
    • Reduction maneuver: accentuate deformity while providing longitudinal traction to "unlock" fracture then rotate distal fragment over proximal fragment into anatomic position
    • Finger-trap traction and assistant counter traction above the elbow can be helpful
    • Cleanse and dress any open wounds
    • Sugar tong splint should then be applied in mild wrist flexion, pronation and ulnar deviation
      • Avoid extreme wrist flexion as it increases carpal tunnel pressure
    • Obtain post-reduction radiographs to confirm acceptable alignment
    • Loss of reduction indicates fracture instability and warrants fixation
    • Acute carpal tunnel syndrome that persists after reduction requires immediate release

  • Acceptable alignment: radial height and volar tilt are most important functionally
    • < 3 mm loss of radial height
    • < 5° change in radial inclination
    • < 10° loss of volar tilt
    • DRUJ reduction
    • < 1-2 mm of articular displacement

  • Nonoperative: indicated for stable fractures in acceptable alignment
    • Accounts for about 75% of fractures
    • Transition from splint to cast after swelling subsides
      • It is currently not clear if a short or long arm cast is preferred
    • Stabilization for 6 weeks to be transitioned to a removable splint for 2-3 more weeks
    • Greater initial displacement, regardless of alignment post-reduction, increases probability of closed treatment failure
    • Older patients who may tolerate malunion may be better served with nonoperative treatment

  • Operative: indications include when closed reduction fails to achieve adequate alignment or when fractures are unstable
    • Goal of fixation is to allow early motion and rehabilitation

    Closed reduction/percutaneous pinning (CRPP): K-wires can be used for minimally comminuted and non-osteoporotic fractures
    • Typically placed from radial styloid to dorsal ulnar aspect of radius to maintain alignment after closed reduction
    • Does have risk of pin site infections, injury to superficial radial nerve and complex regional pain syndrome

    External fixation: considered for fractures that lose reduction in cast treatment or for severe metaphyseal comminution
    • Two pins in 2nd MC and two pins in the radial metaphysis
    • Nonbridging external fixators can also be used to allow early ROM
    • Supplemental percutaneous pin fixation can be utilized
    • Subject to similar infection risks as CRPP

    ORIF: allows direct visualization and manipulation of the fracture
    • Volar plate fixation with angled locked screws/pegs most common
      • Provides easier reduction since volar surface is commonly less comminuted
      • Pronator quadratus provides soft tissue coverage over plate
      • Allows restoration of the thicker volar cortex
    • Dorsal plates can be used but are associated with tendon (extensor pollicis longus) ruptures
      • Allows for buttressing of dorsal rim and dorsally angulated fractures
    • Bone graft may be beneficial for severely comminuted fractures
    • Arthroscopic assistance may improve articular reduction and identification of triangular fibrocartilage complex (TFCC) tears

  • DRUJ treatment after reduction of the distal radius (Fernandez classification):
    Type IA/B: no additional treatment, encourage early pronation/supination
    Type II: may require transfixation of the ulna to the radius with K-wires
    • A: reduce subluxation, ± arthroscopic repair of the TFCC, followed by sugar tong splint immobilization in 45° of supination
    • B: fixation of the ulnar styloid, sugar tong splint immobilization in 45° of supination
    Type IIIA: anatomic reduction of sigmoid notch fragments
    • Residual subluxation should be treated as in type II
    Type IIIB: nonoperative, encourage early pronation/supination

Complications
  • Complication rate 20-27%
  • Compartment syndrome: requires urgent fasciotomy to release pressure and avoid muscle death
  • Complex regional pain syndrome: severe pain and swelling without obvious cause
    • Treat with hand therapy, sympathetic blockade and correction of nerve entrapment
  • Distal radioulnar joint (DRUJ) or triangular fibrocartilage complex (TFCC) injuries
    • Persistent DRUJ instability warrants fixation of the ulnar styloid or repair of the TFCC
  • Malunion: corrective osteotomy is indicated for dorsal angulation > 15°
  • Median nerve: one of the most common complications
    • Mild sensory impairment can be followed clinically
    • Persistent significant neuropathy after reduction should be treated with carpal tunnel release
  • Midcarpal instability:
    • Normally radiolunate angle is 0°, scapholunate angle is 45°
    • Proximal row acts as "intercalated segment" connecting forearm to hand
    • Volar intercalated segment instability (VISI): commonly results from lunotriquetral (LT) dissociation due to LT ligament injury
      • Lunate flexes due to loss of support from triquetrum
      • Scapholunate angle < 30°
      • Radiolunate angle > 15° of flexion
    • Dorsal intercalated segment instability (DISI): commonly due to scaphoid fracture or scapholunate ligament injury
      • Lunate extends due to loss of support from radius (via the scaphoid)
      • Scapholunate angle > 70°
      • Radiolunate angle > 10° extension
    • Intercalated segment instability
  • Nonunion: rare, usually associated with smoking or inadequate immobilization
  • Tendon rupture: extensor pollicis longus most common
    • Can occur even after nondisplaced fracture
    • Due to tendon degeneration after vascular disruption or mechanical impingement due to callus or dorsal plate
    • Suspect when patients report crepitus/pain dorsally with movement of the thumb
