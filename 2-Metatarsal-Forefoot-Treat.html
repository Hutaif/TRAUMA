Treatment
  • Objective is a pain-free foot with normal weight-bearing distribution

  • Fracture treatment by location:
    First metatarsal:
    • Fractures of the 1st MT require more aggressive treatment
      • More likely to displace because it bears more weight
    • Nonoperative: indicated for nondisplaced, stable fractures (as demonstrated by weight-bearing films)
      • SLWC for 4-6 weeks
    • Operative: anatomic reduction is crucial to prevent transfer metarsalgia or painful callouses
      • Goal: restore normal position of the MT head relative to others
      • Simple diaphyseal or articular fractures: percutaneous pinning or screw fixation
      • Transverse or comminuted fractures: plate fixation
      • Severe comminution: external fixation
      • Severe comminution of the 1st metatarsalcuneiform joint may require fusion

    Central metatarsals:
    • Isolated diaphyseal fractures tend to be nondisplaced and can be treated nonoperatively
    • Individual MT head or neck fractures can be reduced to acceptable alignment under hematoma block with longitudinal traction and manipulation
    • 2nd MT base fractures should be stressed for lateral displacement to detect Lisfranc injuries (see Tarsometatarsal Dislocation chapter)
    • Acceptable alignment:
      • Angulation < 10°
      • Translation < 4 mm
    • Nonoperative: indicated for stress fractures or stable fractures in acceptable alignment
      • Hard-soled shoe and WBAT for 3-4 weeks
    • Operative: indications include failed closed reduction and unstable fractures
      • Multiple adjacent MT diaphyseal fractures tend to be unstable and require ORIF
      • Multiple MT head/neck fractures should undergo closed reduction and percutaneous pin fixation

    Fifth metatarsal:
    • Dancer's fracture: symptomatic treatment with hard-soled shoe or walking boot
    • Pseudo-Jones fracture (zone 1): generally symptomatic treatment with a hard-soled shoe or walking boot
      • Displacement > 2 mm or articular involvement may warrant ORIF
    • Jones fracture (zone 2):
      • Nondisplaced fractures can be treated with a SLC, NWB for 6 weeks followed by gradual weight-bearing
      • ORIF with an intramedullary cancellous screw is recommended for displaced fractures and high performance athletes
    • Zone 3 fractures: generally stress fractures
      • Tendency for nonunion requires more aggressive treatment
      • NWB cast for 3 months
      • Failure of casting or chronic fractures require internal fixation with bone grafting

    Phalanges:
    • Majority of phalanx fractures heal nonoperatively
    • Displaced fractures should be reduced under digital block with longitudinal traction
    • Nonoperative: indicated for minimally displaced fractures
      • Rotational alignment must be checked against other toes
      • Stable phalanx fractures can be treated with splinting, buddy taping and/or a hard-soled shoe
      • Immobilization should continue for 3-4 or until symptoms subside
    • Operative: indications include articular fractures of the 1st toe, displaced fractures of the 1st toe and gross instability despite reduction
      • 1st toe proximal phalanx and IP joint injuries require anatomic reduction and fixation
      • Fixation can be achieved with K-wires or screws
      • Capsular or collateral ligament injuries should also be repaired

  • Dislocation treatment by location
    Turf toe:
    • Rest, ice, compression and NSAIDs
    • Protective taping and gradual return to activities
    • Hard-soled shoe with rocker bottom may provide relief
    • Recovery may take 6 weeks
    • Injections of steroid or lidocaine are contraindicated

    1st MTP dislocation:
    • Reduction may be impeded by plantar plate or sesamoids
    • Closed reduction can be perfored under local anesthesia
    • Type I: closed reduction with hyperextension followed by longitudinal traction
    • Type II/III: typically easily reduced by axial traction
    • Nonoperative: indicated for stable joints after closed reduction
      • SLWC with toe extension or hard-soled shoe for 3-4 weeks to allow capsular healing
    • Operative: indications include displaced fractures of the proximal phalanx, osteochondral fragments, irreducible dislocations, post-reduction instabtility and open dislocations
      • Medial incision allows repair of the plantar plate with sutures
      • Phalangeal or metatarsal fractures should be fixed with K-wires or screws
      • Sesamoid fractures can be fixed with sutures
      • Unilateral sesamoid excision is reserved for severe comminution

    Sesamoid fracture:
    • Nonoperative treatment should be attempted first with a heel-bearing SLC for 4-6 weeks
    • Partial sesamoidectomy or bone grafting should be reserved for failed nonoperative treatment (> 6 months)

    Lesser MTP dislocation:
    • Nonoperative: simple dislocations can be reduced by axial traction under local anesthesia
      • Both simple dislocations and minimally displaced fractures can be treated with buddy taping for 4 weeks
      • A rigid shoe can be used to limit MTP motion
    • Operative: indicated for failed closed reductions
      • Block to reduction is typically the plantar plate
      • Open reduction can be achieved through a dorsal incision
      • Temporary pinning to stabilize the joint should be considered

    IP dislocation
    • Reduction of IP joint dislocations can be achieved under digital block with longitudinal traction and manipulation to reverse deformity
    • 1st toe IP joint dislocations are rarely reducible due to tightening of the collaterals and/or interphalangeal sesamoid incarceration
      • Open reduction is often required to remove incarcerated structures
    • Lesser toe dislocations are typically easily reduced and stable after reduction
    • Can be treated with buddy tapping and gradual advancement of activities as tolerated


Complications
  • Compartment syndrome: requires urgent fasciotomies to release pressure and avoid muscle death
  • Delayed union: 38% incidence in nonoperatively treated Jones fractures
  • Malunion: leads to disrupted weight-bearing distribution and transfer metatarsalgia and/or painful keratoses
    • Corrective osteotomies may be required to restore anatomy
  • Nonunion: 14% incidence in nonoperatively treated Jones fractures
  • Posttraumatic arthritis: may result in significant disability when involving the great toe joints
  • Recurrent instability: may require arthrodesis
