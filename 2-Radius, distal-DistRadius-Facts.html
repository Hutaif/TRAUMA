Facts
  • One of the most common orthopedic injuries, representing 15-20% of all fractures
  • 50% are intraarticular
  • Bimodal distribution: young active adults (high-energy) and elderly (high-energy and insufficiency associated with decreased bone mineral density)

  • Distal radius has three independent articular surfaces: scaphoid facet, lunate facet and sigmoid notch
    • Scaphoid and lunate facets separated by an anteroposterior ridge
      • Each facet articulates with its respective carpal bone
    • Sigmoid notch articulates with the distal ulna
      • Located just proximal to the lunate facet on the ulnar aspect of the radius
      • Forms the distal radioulnar joint (DRUJ)
  • The triangular fibrocartilage complex (TFCC) supports the ulnar wrist
    • Components: meniscal homologue, articular disk, ulnocarpal ligaments (ulnolunate and ulnotriquetral) and extensor carpi ulnaris sheath
    • Articulates with the lunate and triquetrum
    • Originates at the ulnar aspect of the radius and attaches at the base (fovea) of the ulnar styloid process
    • Absorbs about 20% of load across wrist joint
    • At higher risk for injury with displaced ulnar styloid fracture
    • Radial deformity, especially shortening, can alter kinematics of the DRUJ and TFCC
  • 80% of axial load is supported by the radius, 20% of axial load is supported by the ulna
    • Small chances in the alignment of the distal radius can result in substantial load transfer to the ulna
  • Ligamentous attachments at the distal radius often remain intact with fracture
    • Facilitates reduction through ligamentotaxis
    • Volar ligaments are stronger than dorsal ligaments
  • Brachioradialis is the only muscle that inserts on the distal radius (radial styloid) and often acts as a deforming force
  • Radius typically fails first volarly in tension followed by compression along the dorsal aspect and comminution depending on energy of trauma
  • Radial shortening results in altered weight-bearing, ulnar impaction pain, grip weakness and loss of forearm rotation
  • Increased dorsal tilt leads to abnormal wrist biomechanics, pain and degeneration at the wrist
    • Can result in dorsal subluxation of the carpus and dorsal intercalated segment instability (DISI) (see Treatment/Complications)

  • Patients present with wrist pain, deformity and swelling
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
    • Examine ipsilateral elbow and shoulder, particularly for radial head and scaphoid injuries
    • Up to 68% of distal radius fractures are associated with soft tissue injuries such as TFCC tears (50%) or disruptions of the scapholunate ligament (18%)
    • Tendon injuries can occur, classically to the extensor pollicis longus
    • Carpal tunnel syndrome occurs in 15% due to either nerve contusion, mechanical deformation or increased pressure
      • Patients typically complain of burning pain in the median distribution
      • Urgent decompression of the carpal tunnel is warranted
  • Compartment syndrome: sustained increase in compartment pressure due to inflammation and edema in forearm compartments leading to vascular compromise and muscle death
    • Signs include pain out of proportion to injury, tense forearm compartments, pain with passive extension or flexion of the digits, diminished pulses
    • Elevate arm and avoid hyperflexion of elbow
    • Serial neurovascular checks for at risk arms
    • Compartment pressure > 30 mmHg or within 30 mmHg of the diastolic blood pressure warrants urgent fasciotomy of volar and dorsal compartments as well as carpal tunnel
