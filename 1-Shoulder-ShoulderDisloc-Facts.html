Facts
  • Most common dislocation
  • Anterior displacement is typical
  • First time dislocators under 20 years of age are at increased risk of recurrent instability
    • Most recurrences occur within the first 2 years

  • Scapulohumeral ligaments
    • Superior glenohumeral ligament (SGHL): originates from superior labrum and coracoid to insert on lesser tuberosity
      • Limits inferior translation and ER in adduction
      • Limits posterior translation in forward flexion, adduction and IR
    • Coracohumeral ligament (CHL): originates from coracoid and inserts on greater and lesser tuberosities
      • Limits inferior translation and ER in adduction
      • Limits posterior translation in forward flexion, adduction and IR
    • Middle glenohumeral ligament (MGHL): originates from the anterior labrum and inserts on the anatomic neck of the humerus
      • Limits ER in adduction
      • Limits inferior translation in adduction and ER
      • Limits anterior and posterior translation in abduction (45°) and ER
      • Buford complex: normal anatomic variant with cord-like MGHL and absent superoanterior labrum
    • Inferior glenohumeral ligament (IGHL): originates from the labrum anteroinferiorly
      • Primary restraint to anterior and inferior instability with shoulder in abduction (45°-90°) and ER
      • Posterior band limits posterior translation in abduction and IR
      • Limits ER in abduction (45°-90°)
      • IGHL must fail for anterior dislocation
      • Bankart lesion: avulsion of the labrum at the IGHL insertion, leading to recurrent anterior instability
      • Humeral avulsions of the IGHL (HAGL) can also occur
  • Rotator interval: region anteriorly between supraspinatus and subscapularis tendons
    • Reinforced by joint capsule, SGHL and CHL
    • Acts to limit excessive flexion, extension, adduction and ER
    • Resists inferior translation in the adducted shoulder
    • Resists posterior translation in the flexed or abducted/ER shoulder
    • Tears of the interval can mimic rotator cuff tears
  • Glenohumeral joint stability is maintained by dynamic and static stabilizers
    • Dynamic: rotator cuff muscles (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, subscapularis and teres minor), pectoralis major, biceps brachii, coracobrachialis and deltoid
      • Generates joint compression force stabilizing the humeral head in the glenoid
      • Infraspinatus, supraspinatus and teres minor insert on the greater tuberosity and resist anterior translation
    • Static: function as primary restraints
      • Includes labrum, articular conformity, capsule, scapulohumeral ligaments and rotator interval
      • Scapulohumeral ligaments per above
      • Posterior capsule and teres minor restrain IR
      • Posteroinferior capsule is primary restraint against posterior dislocation
      • Posterosuperior capsule and SGHL are secondary restraints to posterior dislocation
    • Stabilizers:
      • At 0°: SGHL, CHL and subscapularis
      • At 45°: subscapularis, MGHL and IGHL
      • At 90°: IGHL, biceps, pectoralis and deltoid

  • Determine chronicity, position of shoulder and direction of applied force
    • Recurrent instability related to acquired or congenial laxity may result in anterior dislocation with minimal trauma
  • Examine the seated patient from behind to identify asymmetries between the affected and unaffected shoulder
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries including proximal humerus and glenoid fractures, rotator cuff tears and axillary nerve injuries

    Anterior (95%):
    • High risk of axillary nerve injury (sensation over deltoid laterally)
    • Musculocutaneous nerve can be assessed by sensation along lateral forearm
    • Hill-Sachs lesions (posterolateral humeral head impaction) are present in 27% of acute and 74% of recurrent dislocations but visualized in only 50% of AP radiographs
    • Associated with labral, rotator cuff and capsular tears, glenoid labral articular disruptions (GLAD), glenoid and greater tuberosity fractures
    • Anterior band of the IGHL is necessarily injured
    • Typically presents with arm in abduction and ER, a palpable mass anteriorly, prominence of the acromion and a hollow beneath the acromion posteriorly
    • Apprehension sign: in the reduced shoulder, reproduce sense of instability with abduction, ER and extension
      • Mitigate apprehension with posteriorly directed pressure on anterior shoulder

    Posterior (2%):
    • 60% are missed on initial examination
    • Posterior dislocations are classically due to seizure (external rotators are stronger)
    • Axillary nerve injury can occur (less common than with anterior dislocation)
    • Impaction of the anterior humeral head (reverse Hill-Sachs) is present in 75%
    • Associated with posterior labral tears (reverse Bankart), disruption of the posterior band of the IGHL, teres minor tears and posterior glenoid fractures
    • Patients can present without obvious deformity but typically demonstrate limited ER (0°) and forward flexion (< 90°)

    Inferior (1%):
    • Rare
    • Known as "luxatio erecta"
    • Caused by hyperabduction and levering on acromion
    • Associated with pectoralis and rotator cuff tears, greater tuberosity, acromion and glenoid rim fractures
    • Axillary artery or brachial plexus injuries can occur
    • Articular surface faces inferiorly with humerus extending overhead causing patients to present in a "salute" posture
    • Neurovascular compromise is common with luxatio erecta but usually resolves with reduction

    Superior (rare):
    • Rare
    • Associated with fractures of the acromion, clavicle, AC joint, coracoid and humeral tuberosities, rotator cuff and biceps tears
    • Usually presents with shortened arm in adduction
    • Palpable humeral head above the acromion
