Facts
  • Metatarsal (MT) fractures are commonly due to a direct blow or twisting mechanism
  • Phalanx fractures are the most common fracture of the forefoot
    • 1st and 5th digits are involved most frequently
    • Usually due to a crush or stub mechanism
  • Dislocations of the IP and MTP joints are commonly due to dorsiflexion and axial loading

  • Forefoot (all structures distal to the TMT joint) provides a broad surface for weight-bearing
    • The four lesser MTs and the 1st MT (with its two sesmoids) support half of the forefoot load during normal gait
  • Metatarsophalangeal (MTP) joint motion is essential for comfort and normal gait
  • Mobility in the sagittal plane allows accommodation to uneven surfaces
    • Allows relatively even pressure distribution
  • Alterations in structure affect this loading pattern
    • Dorsal displacement can lead to overload of adjacent MTs and transfer metatarsalgia (pain under the MT heads, typically the 2nd or 3rd)
    • Plantar displacement can lead to plantar keratoses and pain
  • Transverse MT ligaments at the neck and intermetatarsal ligaments proximally connect lesser MTs to each other
    • Dense ligamentous attachments proximally anchor MTs to the tarsal bones
      • TMT joint motion increases from 2nd to 5th MC
    • Due to these structures, isolated MT fractures rarely displace
  • 1st and 5th MT provide protection and stability to others
  • Stress fractures are common at the necks of the 2nd/3rd and proximal 5th MCs
  • Foot compartments:
    • Medial: abductor hallucis, flexor hallucis brevis
    • Interosseous: 7 interossei muscles
    • Central superficial: flexor digitorum brevis, adductor hallucis, lumbricals
    • Central deep (calcaneal): quadratus plantae
    • Lateral: flexor digiti minimi brevis, abductor digiti minimi, opponens digiti minimi

  • Patients typically present with pain, visible deformity and variable swelling
  • Point tenderness, crepitus and deformity are consistent with fractures
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
  • Compartment syndrome: sustained increase in compartment pressure due to inflammation and edema in foot compartments leading to vascular compromise and muscle death
    • Crush injuries increase risk
    • Signs include tense swelling, diminished pulses, and pain out of proportion to injury, particularly with passive extension of the digits
    • Compartment pressures should be measured when signs of compartment syndrome are present
    • Urgent fasciotomies of the compartments are warranted for pressures within 30 mmHg of the diastolic blood pressure

    First metatarsal:
    • Lack of interconnecting ligaments with other MTs allows independent motion
    • Tibialis anterior inserts on the medial plantar base (extends)
    • Peroneus longus inserts on the lateral plantar base (flexes)
    • Two sesmoids within the flexor hallucis brevis (FHB) tendon lie under the head and share in weight-bearing
    • More often open or comminuted fractures due to higher energy injuries
    • Crucial in weight-bearing: anatomic reduction is necessary

    Central metatarsals:
    • Transverse metatarsal ligament connects the MTs at their heads
    • Tarsometatarsal joints increase in mobility from 2nd to 5th
    • Evaluate for Lisfranc injury (weight-bearing films), especially with involvement of the base of the 2nd MC

    Fifth metatarsal:
    • Peroneus brevis inserts on the dorsum of the tubercle
    • Peroneus tertius inserts on the dorsum of the proximal diaphysis
    • Os peroneum: an ossicle that may be present lateral to the cuboid within the peroneus longus tendon
    • Os vesalianum: an accessory bone that may be present medial to the insertion of the peroneus brevis tendon
    • Proximal diaphyseal-metaphyseal region is a vascular watershed zone at risk for delayed union or nonunion

    First MTP:
    • Normal ROM: 75° of dorsiflexion and 35° of plantarflexion
    • Strong constraints: dorsal capsule/extensor hallucis longus, plantar plate/flexor hallucis longus (FHL), flexor hallucis brevis (FHB), medial/lateral collateral ligaments as well as conformity of the convex proximal phalanx and the concave metatarsal head
    • Turf toe: hyperextension sprain of the 1st MTP with injury to the plantar plate and capsule
    • Dislocations are rare and typically result from high-energy trauma
      • Commonly associated with other injuries to the foot
      • Usually dorsal with proximal phalanx displaced proximally
    • Articular injury may result in hallux rigidus
    • Test stability against contralateral side, particularly translation with dorsoplantar stress
      • Significant difference between sides implies ligamentous disruption

    Sesamoids:
    • Two sesamoids are part of the plantar plate complex of the 1st MTP joint
    • They lie in the FHB tendon at the level of the 1st MTP with the FHL running between them
    • Intersesamoid ligaments connects the two sesamoids
    • Serve as shock absorbers and as a fulcrum for the 1st toe
    • Stress fractures are the result of repetitive hyperextension as in ballet and runners or acute fracture due to hyperextension and flexor tension
    • Medial sesamoid is more frequently fractured due to increased weight-bearing
    • Bipartite sesamoids are common (10-30%),
      • Up to 85% are bilateral
      • More commonly observed in the medial sesamoid
      • Can be differentiated from fracture by smooth, sclerotic edges
    • Osteochondritis dissecans: avascular subchondral bone necrosis
      • Can be differentiated from a fracture by appearance of fragmentation and irregularity
    • Patients present with point tenderness under the sesamoid
    • Dorsiflexion of the 1st MTP can exacerbate the pain

    Lesser MTPs: generally suffer injuries from stubbing mechanisms (hyperdorsiflexion)
    • Most common in 5th MTP
    • Stability conferred by articular congruity, plantar capsule, transverse MT ligament, tendons and lumbricals
    • Dislocations usually dorsal
    • Typically present with dorsal foot pain
    • Examination usually reveals disruption in the normal cascade of the toes

    IP joint dislocations: usually involve the PIP joint
    • Typically dorsal in direction
    • Most commonly occurs in 1st toe
    • Proximal/distal IP joint motion of the lesser toes is not crucial to gait or foot function
    • Patients present with an obviously deformed, cocked-up toe

    Phalanx:
    • 1st toe phalangeal alignment is important to prevent symptomatic arthrosis
    • Most lesser toe fractures heal well with symptomatic treatment
    • A subungual hematoma may indicate distal phalanx fracture
