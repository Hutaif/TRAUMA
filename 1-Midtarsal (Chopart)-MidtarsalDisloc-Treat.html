Treatment
  • Objective: to restore anatomic alignment and stability
  • Closed reduction can be achieved following application of adequate analgesia
    • Longitudinal traction and manual pressure to reverse deformity depending on direction of dislocation
    • Generally less successful for fracture-dislocations
  • Following reduction, weight-bearing films should be obtained to evaluate instability and dictate definitive treatment

    Pure ligamentous dislocations:
  • Nonoperative indicated for anatomic reduction with minimal instability
    • NWB SLC immobilization
  • Operative: indications include failed closed reduction and instability
    • Anatomic reduction but weight-bearing instability: transarticular percutaneous fixation is warranted
    • Joint asymmetry and evidence of instability: ORIF with K-wire or bridge plating
    • Arthrodesis may be required to prevent progressive collapse in highly unstable cases

    Fracture-dislocations:
  • Nonoperative: indicated for avulsion fractures and nondisplaced fractures that do not demonstrate instability on weight-bearing films
    • Avulsion fractures: nonrigid dressing and protected weight-bearing for 4-6 weeks
    • Nondisplaced fracture: SLC and NWB for 6 weeks
  • Operative: indications include displaced fractures, failed closed reduction, incarcerated fragments and open fracture
    • ORIF technique depends on bones affected (see respective fracture chapters)
    • Consider bone grafting to reconstruct column length
    • Arthrodesis with or without bridge plating may be necessary in setting of severe comminution

Complications
  • Compartment syndrome: requires urgent fasciotomies to release pressure and avoid muscle death
  • Posttraumatic arthritis: a consequence of any articular injury, can be theoretically minimized with anatomic reduction
    • Intractable symptoms may require arthrodesis
