Treatment
  • Goal is anatomic restoration of the articular surface to prevent posttraumatic arthritis
  • Skeletal traction should be applied initially for any gross femoral head/acetabulum incongruity to reduce femoral head (minimize risk osteonecrosis and pressure necrosis of articular cartilage) and permit soft tissue healing while other injuries are addressed
  • Surgery should be performed within the first 10 days to ensure mobile bone fragments
  • Instability is most common with posterior types but large quadrilateral plate fractures may result in medial subluxation (central instability) of the femoral head

  • Nonoperative: indicated for < 2 mm displacement, concentric/stable reduction of the femoral head, absence of incarcerated bone fragments, low anterior column or infratectal transverse fractures where concentric reduction of the femoral head is maintained, acceptable roof arc angles (see Imaging), both column fractures with secondary congruence (relatively congruent relationship between femoral head and fractured articular surface of the acetabulum) or patients who cannot tolerate surgery
    • Protected weight-bearing based on the fracture pattern

  • Operative: indications include fragments displaced > 2 mm, incarcerated fragments, articular incongruity and hip instability
    • Fixation achieved using lag screws and pelvic reconstruction plates
    • Surgical approaches, depending on fracture:
      • Kocher-Langenbeck (posterior): most appropriate for posterior column, posterior wall and some transverse patterns
        - Does not allow direct access to the anterior wall or column
      • Ilioinguinal or Stoppa (anterior): most appropriate for anterior column, anterior wall and some both column patterns
        - Visualizes SI joint and inner table of the ilium
        - Does not allow intraarticular visualization of the hip
      • Extended iliofemoral: most appropriate for both column and some T-type fractures
        - Associated with high incidence of heterotopic ossification (up to 35%)
    • Postoperatively:
      • Thromboembolic prophylaxis
      • Consider indomethacin/irradiation for heterotopic ossification prophylaxis in posterior and extended iliofemoral approaches
      • Weight-bearing is delayed for 8-12 weeks and until radiographic evidence of healing

Complications
  • Femoral nerve injury: traction injury with ilioinguinal approach
  • Heterotopic ossification: highest with extended iliofemoral approach
    • Consider perioperative indomethecin or radiation
  • Lateral femoral cutaneous nerve injury: 12% rate of neuralgia after anterior exposures
  • Osteonecrosis (2-4%): associated with femoral head dislocation at the time of injury
  • Posttraumatic arthritis: timely concentric reduction and removal of incarcerated fragments can minimize this risk
  • Sciatic nerve injury: associated with posterior hip dislocations and prolonged traction in the Kocher-Langenbeck approach
    • Keep hip extended, knee flexed during surgery to reduce risk
  • Superior gluteal nerve injury: most vulnerable as it exits the greater sciatic foramen
  • Surgical wound infections (2-5%): increased risk with abdominal/visceral injuries, extensile exposures and obesity
  • Thromboembolic disease:
    • Deep venous thrombosis: occurs in 61% of patients with pelvic trauma
    • Pulmonary embolism: occurs in 10% of patients with major trauma
