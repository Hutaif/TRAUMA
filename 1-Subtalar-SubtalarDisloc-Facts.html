Facts
  • Involves dislocation of the talocalcaneal and talonavicular joints
  • Uncommon injury that usually occurs in young adults after fall from height and MVCs
  • Medial dislocations of the calcaneus/foot are most common (80%)
    • Due to plantarflexion and inversion of the forefoot
    • Can lead to buttonholing of the talar head through the anterior capsule and interposition of the extensor digitorum brevis, preventing reduction in up to 10% of cases
  • Lateral dislocations of the calcaneus/foot (20%)
    • Due to plantarflexion and eversion of the forefoot
    • Can lead to buttonholing of the talar head through the anterior capsule and interposition of the posterior tibial tendon, preventing reduction in up to 20% of cases
    • Generally due to higher energy and has a worse prognosis than medial dislocations

  • Patients present with obvious deformity and/or swelling and inability to bear weight
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
  • Other injuries around the foot and ankle are very common
  • High energy nature of injury may warrant full trauma work-up
  • Open dislocations can occur in up to 40% of patients
