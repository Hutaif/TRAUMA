Treatment
  • Consider joint aspiration to relieve discomfort and facilitate examination of ROM
    • Identify the triangle formed by the lateral epicondyle, lateral olecranon and the radial head
    • The soft spot in the center of this triangle is your entry point for aspiration (anconeus then capsule then joint space)
    • Injection of local anesthetic can facilitate exam

  • Operative treatment:
    • Open reduction through a Kocher (lateral) incision or a posterior incision
    • Fixation with miniature lag screws, differential pitch screws (e.g. Herbert) or bioabsorbable pins
    • Fractures deemed not reconstructable can be replaced with metalic implants

  • Treatment by injury:
    Mason I:
    • Symptomatic: sling, early ROM (< 7 days) and protected weight-bearing for 6 weeks

    Mason II:
    • If supination/pronation is limited, aspirate joint followed by injection of local anesthetic to assess mechanical block
      • If no mechanical block: symptomatic treatment with sling and early ROM per Mason I
      • If mechanical block: ORIF
        - If fragment is too small for stable fixation (roughly < 25% of the articular surface area), excision of fragment is appropriate
    • Displacement > 2 mm with associated elbow instability warrants ORIF

    Mason III:
    • Fractures without mechanical block, associated fractures (e.g. coronoid) or instability can be considered for nonsurgical treatment per Mason I
    • If a mechanical block exists, the radial head must be fixed or excised and replaced
    • Replacement is indicated for displaced, comminuted fractures when reconstruction of at least 50% of the articular surface area is not feasible
      • Replacement of the radial head appears to produce the best results
    • Excision alone is associated with late complications and is contraindicated in cases with concomitant MCL or IOM injury
      • Reserved for elderly, low demand patients
    • Ligamentous injuries must be addressed simultaneously to restore stability (as per terrible triad below)

    Mason IV:
    • Treat dislocation acutely (see Elbow Dislocation chapter) and radial head fracture per above

    Terrible Triad: see Elbow Dislocation chapter for more information
    • Nonoperative: indicated for concentric reduction, stability at 30° of flexion or less, minimally displaced radial head fracture (by CT) without block to rotation and Type I coronoid (by CT)
      • Immobilize in splint or cast for 7-10 days followed by early ROM
    • Operative: generally preferred
      • Usually through a posterior incision
      • Fix coronoid fracture with sutures (Type I/II) or screw (Type III)
      • Fix or replace radial head as described above
      • Repair LCL to lateral epicondyle with suture anchors or drill holes
      • MCL may need to be repaired if instability persists after coronoid and lateral repair
      • An external fixator may be required for residual instability
      • Immobilize for 7-10 days followed by early ROM

    Essex-Lopresti lesion:
    • Reduce DRUJ with full supination of the forearm
    • Percutaneous pinning of the DRUJ for 3-4 weeks can be used to maintain reduction
    • Fix or replace radial head as described above
    • Avoid silastic prostheses (prone to mechanical failure) and radial head excision (further destabilizes radius longitudinally)
    • Repair of the TFCC and IOM may be necessary in the non-acute setting

Complications
  • Posttraumatic arthritis: a consequence of any articular injury, can be theoretically minimized with anatomic reduction
    • Associated with prosthetic replacements
  • Elbow stiffness: associated with prolonged immobilization
  • Essex-Lopresti: chronic wrist pain may represent unrecognized intraosseous membrane injury
  • Posterior interosseous nerve injury: as a result of operative treatment
    • Reduce of injury by performing lateral approach with forearm pronated
  • Radial head resection: can lead to joint instability, proximal radial translation, cubitus valgus, elbow and wrist pain
  • Reflex sympathetic dystrophy: can develop after surgical management
  • Unrecognized dislocation: may lead to persistent instability and late redislocation
