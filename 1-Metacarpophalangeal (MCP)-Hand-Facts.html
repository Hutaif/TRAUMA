Facts
  • Fractures are commonly work related, due to either direct blow or a fall
    • Border digits most commonly involved
    • Phalangeal fractures twice as common as metacarpal (MC)
    • Proximal phalanx most common, followed by distal
  • Dislocations of the interphalangeal (IP) joints are commonly due to ball sports and primarily dorsal
  • Dislocations of the metacarpalphalangeal (MCP) joints are usually dorsal

  • Extensor digitorum communis (EDC) extends the MCP and IP joints, attaching via three slips:
    • Sagittal bands: arise from the volar plate and centralize the EDC over the MCP joint
      • EDC extends MCP via the sagittal bands
    • Central slip: EDC attachment to the dorsum of the middle phalanx
    • Lateral bands: continuation of the EDC that inserts on the base of the distal phalanx
  • Flexor digitorum profundus (FDP) inserts on the base of the distal phalanx
  • Flexor digitorum superficialis (FDS) bifurcates around the FDP and inserts on the base of the middle phalanx
  • Flexor tendons travel through a sheath that protects, lubricates and nourishes
    • Pulleys: aponeurotic bands along the volar surface provide a mechanical advantage and prevent bowstringing
      • A1, A3 and A5 are volar to the MCP, PIP and DIP respectively
      • A2 and A4 are volar to the proximal and middle phalanges (most important mechanically)
    • Extensor tendons do not have a sheath
    • Patients presenting after a puncture injury can develop an infection of the flexor tendon sheath (infectious tenosynovitis)
      • These patients classicaly present with Kanavel's cardinal signs:
        1. Flexed posture
        2. Fusiform swelling along digit
        3. Tenderness to percussion
        4. Pain with passive extension of the digit
      • Presence of these four signs warrants open irrigation and debridement
  • Lumbricals arise from the FDP and insert on the extensor expansion/lateral bands of the EDC
    • Flexes the metacarpophalangeal (MCP) through its insertion distal to the MCP
    • Extends proximal interphalangeal (PIP) joints through its insertion on the lateral bands
    • Only muscle that inserts on its own antagonist
  • Interossei arise from the MC and insert on the extensor expansion
    • Palmar interossei adduct while dorsal interossei abduct the digits
  • Extrinisic muscles arise in the forearm and insert within the hand
    • These include the EDC, FDS and FDP
  • Intrinsic muscles: arise and insert within the hand
    • These include the lumbricals and interossei
  • Volar plate: fibrocartilaginous sheet that forms the floor of the IP and MCP joints
  • Hand compartments: dorsal interossei (4), palmar interossei (3), adductor pollicis, thenar and hypothenar

  • Patients often present with deformity and swelling
  • Pertinent patient information includes: age, hand dominance, occupation, systemic illnesses, nature of injury, time of injury (relevant for open injuries), exposure to contamination, work related injury
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
  • Compartment syndrome: sustained increase in compartment pressure due to inflammation and edema in one or more of the hand compartments leading to vascular compromise and muscle death
    • Signs include tense swelling, pain out of proportion to injury and loss of motion
    • Sensory exam may be normal
    • Hand may assume intrinsic minus position (MCP extension, IP flexion)
    • Compartment pressures must be measured when clinical signs of compartment syndrome are present
    • Pressure > 15-20 mmHg warrants release of all 10 compartments
      • Transverse carpal ligament requires release as well

    Metacarpal:
  • Unlike phalanges, have unicondular head
  • Deep transverse ligament limits translation of the middle digit MCs
  • Due to interossei, most neck and shaft fractures angulate apex dorsal
  • Proper rotational reduction can be confirmed by evaluating the digit cascade
    • With the forearm upright, the digits fall into passive flexion
    • All digits should point towards the scaphoid
    • Compare overlap with uninjured side
    • Excess overlap indicates malrotation

    Metacarpophalangeal joint
  • Fingers:
    • Ulnar and radial collateral ligaments are primary stabilizers
      • Tighter in flexion than extension
      • Volar plate stabilizes the volar aspect
    • The MCP is weakest dorsally, thus in most dislocations the proximal phalanx displaces dorsally
    • MC head may buttonhole volarly between the natatory ligament and the superficial transverse MC ligament preventing reduction
    • Irreducible dislocations can also result when volar plate becomes incarcerated within the joint (volar plate remains attached to proximal phalanx)
    • Stress testing should be performed in flexion, when collateral ligaments are on stretch
  • Thumb:
    • Ulnar collateral ligament (UCL) is crucial for function (pincer grasp)
      • Skier's or Gamekeeper's thumb: acute injury to the UCL
    • Stener lesion: adductor pollicis aponeurosis interposition between the two ends of a ruptured UCL can prevent healing
      • Can be diagnosed by a tender, palpable mass over the UCL
    • Thumb MCP has sesmoids embedded in the volar plate
      • Flexor pollicis brevis (FPB) and abductor policis brevis partially insert on the sesamoids
      • Presence of a sesamoid within a widened thumb MCP joint is pathognomonic for an irreducible joint

    Proximal phalanx
  • Anatomic regions: head, neck, shaft and base
  • Bicondylar head
  • Fractures tend to deform apex volar
  • Complex extensor mechanism surrounds this bone and must be considered during surgical approaches

    Proximal interphalangeal joint
  • Collateral ligament and volar plate disruptions are common
  • Central slip injury can result in a Boutonnière deformity if not treated
  • Evaluate extensor mechanism with active extension from a flexed position
  • Dislocations my be irreducible due to volar plate or flexor tendon incarceration

    Middle phalanx
  • Anatomic regions: head, neck, shaft and base
  • Central slip attaches dorsally at the base
  • FDS attaches along volar surface
  • Distal fractures tend to angulate apex volar
  • Proximal fracture tend to angulate apex dorsal

    Distal and thumb interphalangeal joint
  • Bicondylar joint that is stabilized by collateral ligaments
    • Dynamic balance between flexor and extensor tendons provide additional stability
  • Transverse wounds along the volar skin crease are common and require evaluation and treatment
  • Irreducible dislocations can occur for various reasons:
    • Dorsal: incarceration of the volar plate or buttonholing of the middle phalanx through the FDP
    • Volar: incarceration of the extensor tendon
    • Thumb: incarceration of the sesamoids or volar plate

    Distal phalanx
  • Most offten fractured in a crush or axial load
  • Anatomic regions: tuft (distal), shaft and base
  • Fractures often also have nailbed injuries that require repair
