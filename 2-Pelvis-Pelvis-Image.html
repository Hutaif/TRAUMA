Imaging
    Radiographs
  • AP pelvis: identify pubic rami fractures, pubic symphysis diastasis, sacroiliac joint diastasis, sacral fractures, iliac fractures, L5 transverse process fractures
    • Normal AP pelvis
  • Judet views (obturator and iliac oblique views) for suspected acetabular fractures
  • Inlet: supine with beam 25-45° caudal
    • Identify anterior/posterior displacement of the sacrum or iliac wing as well as rotational deformity
  • Outlet: supine with beam 45-60° cephalad
    • Identify vertical displacement of the hemipelvis
    • Note fractures of the sacrum, disruption of the sacral foramina or widening of the sacroiliac joints
  • Lateral sacrum: screen for sacral fractures
  • Ferguson view: modified outlet view that is a true AP of the sacrum
  • Radiographic signs of instability: 5 mm of posterior sacroiliac displacement, avulsion fracture of the ischial spine, ischial tuberosity, sacrum or the L5 transverse process

  • CT: more accurate in identifying presence, pattern and location of fractures
    • Contrast can be used to identify active hemorrhage and colonic injury
