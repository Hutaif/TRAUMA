Facts
  • Usually the result of a direct blow, MVC or falls
  • Isolated radius fractures are rare

  • Radial bow is critical to allow for supination and pronation
  • Interosseous membrane (IOM) is important for longitudinal stability
    • Fibrous band that connects the radius to the ulna along most of their length
    • Separates the dorsal and volar forearm compartments
    • Middle third is the strongest
  • Radial fractures between the supinator and pronator teres insertions cause supination of the proximal fragment
    • Radial fractures distal to both result in neutral rotation
  • Monteggia lesion: fracture of proximal ulna with radial head dislocation
    • Usually result from a fall on an outstretched hand with a flexed and pronated elbow
    • Increased risk of radial or posterior interosseous nerve (PIN) injuries
      • PIN is the deep motor branch of the radial nerve and supplies all the wrist extensors other than the ECRL
  • Nightstick fracture: isolated diaphyseal ulna fracture from trauma to the subcutaneous border
  • Distal third radius fractures must be evaluated for distal radioulnar joint (DRUJ) injuries
  • Galeazzi fracture: fracture of the radius between middle and distal third with associated DRUJ and IOM disruption
    • Three times more common than Monteggia lesion
  • Reverse Galeazzi: fracture of the distal ulna with associated DRUJ disruption
  • Compartment syndrome: sustained increase in compartment pressure due to inflammation and edema in forearm compartments leading to vascular compromise and muscle death
    • Signs include pain out of proportion to injury, tense forearm compartments, pain with passive extension or flexion of the digits, diminished pulses
    • Elevate arm and avoid hyperflexion of elbow
    • Serial neurovascular checks for at risk arms
    • Compartment pressure > 30 mmHg or within 30 mmHg of the diastolic blood pressure warrants urgent fasciotomy of volar and dorsal compartments as well as carpal tunnel

  • Patients present with pain, swelling, deformity, tenderness to palpation and loss of function of the forearm and hand
  • Diagnosis is generally straightforward as nondisplaced fractures are relatively uncommon
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
  • Monteggia fractures at risk for PIN injury
    • PIN can be tested by examining extension of the thumb (EPL) and digits (EDC) with the wrist in neutral
  • Galeazzi fracture patients typically present with wrist pain that is exacerbated by stressing the DRUJ
    • Radial head reduction must be verified
