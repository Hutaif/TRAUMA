Facts
  • Orthopedic emergency: potential limb threatening injury due to popliteal artery thrombosis/disruption
  • Vascular disruption is reported in 10-65% of cases
  • Obese patients with low-energy dislocations are at highest risk for vascular injury
  • Hyperextension injuries typically result in anterior dislocation
  • Dashboard injuries with the knee in flexion typically result in posterior dislocation

  • Normal ROM: 10° of extension to 140° of flexion
  • Knee has three compartments: patellofemoral, medial tibiofemoral and lateral tibiofemoral
  • Menisci in both tibiofemoral compartments decrease stress by increasing conformity
  • Femoral condyles articulate with the tibia
    • The medial condyle extends further distally than the lateral, contributing to the physiologic 6° of valgus at the knee
    • The medial tibial plateau is flat and larger than the lateral plateau which is concave
  • The proximal tibial has 7°-10° of posterior slope
  • Gerdy's tubercle (lateral) is the insertion of the iliotibial (IT) band
  • Anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) arises from the posterolateral femoral notch and inserts anterior to the tibial eminences
    • Resists anterior translation of the tibia
  • Posterior cruciate ligament (PCL) arises from the anteromedial femoral notch and inserts on the posterior tibia
    • Resists posterior translation of the tibia
  • Knee has three layers:
    • Layer I: fascial layer that includes the sartorious fascia (medial), the IT band and biceps femoris fascia (lateral)
    • Layer II: patellar tendon, superficial MCL and LCL
    • Layer III: deep MCL, fabellofibular and arcuate ligaments (lateral)
  • Baker's cyst: posterior synovial connection to a popliteal bursa between the semimembranosus and medial gastrocnemius
  • Dislocations typically result from disruption of at least one cruciate ligament and variable disruption of collateral ligaments, capsule and menisci
  • Popliteal vessels emerge from adductor hiatus to enter the popliteal fossa
    • Five geniculate branches supply the knee
    • Proceeds under soleus through a fibrous tunnel
    • Results in tethering proximally and distally that puts popliteal vessels at risk
  • Anterior and posterior dislocations occur equally, each accounting for 30-40%

  • Patients with knee dislocations present with deformity, severe pain and inability to ambulate
    • Spontaneous reduction can occur, resulting in more subtle signs of injury at presentation (e.g. effusion, laxity, etc.)
  • Immediate knee reduction should be performed
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
    • Most common finding in patients with vascular injury is diminished or absent pulses
      • Additional findings include active hemorrhage, expanding hematoma (popliteal fossa), distal ischemia, bruit
      • Consider noninvasive Doppler studies for any equivocal findings
      • Angiography should be performed in patients with abnormal vascular examination
      • Gross vascular disruption should proceed directly to the operating room
      • Flow must be reestablished within 6-8 hours
    • Evaluate knee for open wounds
    • Peroneal nerve injury (10-40%) associated with posterolateral dislocations
    • Note other fractures about the knee including tibial eminence, tibial tubercle and proximal fibula
  • A patient presenting with a knee reduced in the field and history of acute dislocation remains at risk for vascular injury and should be evaluated equally urgently
  • Patient should be monitored closely for 1 week for delayed presentation of vascular injury (e.g. intimal tears, late thrombosis)
  • Compartment syndrome: sustained increase in compartment pressure due to inflammation and edema in one or more of the leg compartments leading to vascular compromise and muscle death
    • Muscle death occurs 6-8 hours after the onset of compartment syndrome
    • Severe pain with passive motion of the toes/foot is often first sign
    • Other signs include tense swelling of the calf, diminished pulses, pain out of proportion to injury and paresthesias
      • Serial neurovascular exams are required as compartment syndrome can develop more than 24 hours after injury
    • Compartment pressures should be measured in patients with clincal signs of compartment syndrome
      • Compartment pressure > 30 mmHg or within 30 mmHg of the diastolic blood pressure warrant urgent fasciotomies of the leg compartments
    • Deep posterior compartment pressure may be elevated in the presence of a soft superficial compartment
      • Missed posterior compartment syndrome can result in claw toe deformity
