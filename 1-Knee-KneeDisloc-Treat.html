Treatment
  • Surgical treatment results in significantly improved ROM, knee function and reduced contractures compared to nonoperative treatment

  • Closed reduction: immediate reduction should be performed upon presentation with axial traction and translation to reverse deformity
    • Splint knee in 20°-30° of flexion
    • Dislocations with a rotational component may require open reduction due to buttonholing of the femoral condyle through the knee capsule

  • Nonoperative: may be indicated for morbid obesity, sedentary lifestyle, low demand elderly patients or patients with ACL/MCL injuries and minimal posterior laxity (intact PCL)
    • Closed reduction followed by a short period of immobilization with early ROM and physical therapy

  • Operative: indications include failed closed reduction, soft tissue interposition, open or vascular injuries
    • Knee spanning external fixation is required for vascular repair (typically saphoneous vein graft from contralateral leg) or open dislocations
      • High amputation rates for vascular repair delayed > 8 hours
      • Vascular repair delays > 6 hours should undergo leg fasciotomy
    • Examine knee ligaments under anesthesia if possible

  • Subacute repair of knee ligaments:
    • In general, early surgical intervention (< 4 weeks) followed by aggressive rehabilitation to prevent arthrofibrosis is advocated
    • Large bony avulsion fragments benefit from acute (< 1 week) fixation
    • Multi-ligament injury with disruption to the lateral structures (LCL, popliteus, lateral capsule, popliteofibular ligament) warrants treatment within 2 weeks (before the onset of significant scarring)
    • ACL tears with intact PCL: delayed reconstruction (6 weeks) of the ACL to allow early ROM appears to decrease arthrofibrosis
    • PCL tears with intact ACL: early reconstruction of the PCL appears to be advantageous
    • Both ACL and PCL tears: early reconstruction of both or staged PCL then ACL is generally recommended
    • Injuries to the posteromedial or posterolateral corners warrant repair or reconstruction in most cases

Complications
  • Compartment syndrome: requires urgent fasciotomy to release pressure and avoid muscle death
  • Heterotopic ossification (26-43%): correlated with Injury Severity Score
  • Knee stiffness: most common
    • Due to scarring and capsular tightness
    • Lysis of adhesions may be performed
  • Ligamentous laxity: uncommon after adequate immobilization and reconstruction
  • Peroneal nerve injury (10-40%): recovery rates reported around 35%
  • Vascular disruption (10-65%)
