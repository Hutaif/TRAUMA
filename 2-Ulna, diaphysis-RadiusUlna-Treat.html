Treatment
  • Fixation techniques:
    • Compression plating: allows compression across fracture site, facilitating healing
      • Preferred for most fractures
    • Locked plating: screws thread into plate at a fixed angle creating a rigid construct that does not rely on bone-plate interface friction
      • Useful for comminuted or osteoporotic fractures
    • Intramedullary nailing: alternative to plating for pathologic and segmental fractures
      • Requires cast immobilization
    • External fixation: temporizing fixation in polytrauma patients or in severely contaminated wounds
    • Radius can be approached via a Henry anterior (extensile) or Thompson posterolateral (middle third) depending on location of fracture
    • Ulna can be approached directly posteriorly
    • To protect the PIN in proximal third radius fractures, anterior approaches should be performed in supination, posterior approaches should be performed in pronation

    Both bone:
  • Nonoperative: indicated for nondisplaced fractures
    • Well-molded LAC with neutral forearm rotation and elbow flexed at 90°
    • Follow radiographically each week
  • Operative: indicated for displaced fractures
    • Fixation per above

    Nightstick:
  • Nonoperative: indicated for stable fractures (< 10° of angulation and < 50% displacement)
    • Sugar tong splint for 1-2 weeks then functional bracing for 6 weeks
  • Operative: indicated for unstable fractures (> 10° of angulation or > 50% displacement)
    • Fixation per above

    Monteggia:
  • Commonly associated with radial head fracture (see Radial Head Fracture chapter)
  • Nonoperative: only in the pediatric population
  • Operative:
    • Closed reduction under anesthesia followed by ORIF of ulna typically with a compression plate
    • Elbow and proximal radioulnar joint should be assessed intraoperatively since malreduction of the fracture can lead to persistent instability of the radial head
    • Anatomic fracture reduction with persistent radial head subluxation should raise suspicion of joint incarceration of the capsule, annular ligament or osteochondral fragment
    • Posterior splint for 5-6 days followed by early motion

    Radius, diaphysis:
  • Rare in isolation
  • Nonoperative: indicated for nondisplaced fractures
    • LAC with the forearm in neutral to full rotation (depending on the location of the fracture) and the elbow in 90°
  • Operative: generally preferred to attain proper radial bow
    • Fixation per above

    Galeazzi:
  • 92% failure rate with closed treatment
    • Weight of the hand results in angulation of the fracture and subluxation of DRUJ
    • Pronator quadratus insertion tends to pronate the distal fragment with proximal and volar displacement
    • Brachioradialis tends to shorten fracture
    • Thumb extensors and abductors lead to shortening despite immobilization
  • Operative: generally preferred
    • Plate fixation of radius followed by intraoperative evaluation of DRUJ stability
    • DRUJ instability is typically dorsal
    • Consider open or arthoscopic evaluation of the joint followed by K-wire fixation
    • If the DRUJ is stable, post-operative plaster fixation may be sufficient
  • Sugar tong splint in supination to minimize tension for 2 weeks

Complications
  • Compartment syndrome: requires urgent fasciotomy to release pressure and avoid muscle death
  • Infection (1-7%): deep infections require debridement and IV antibiotics
  • Malunion: loss of radial bow can impede forearm rotation
    • May require corrective osteotomy and compression plating
  • Nonunion (2%): ulnar fractures tend to heal more slowly due to higher percentage of cortical bone
    • Infected nonunions require segmental resection and reconstruction after elimination of infection
  • Radioulnar synostosis (2%): increased risk with open fractures, infection, bone graft, crush and head injuries
    • Treat with resection, early motion and prophylaxis (radiation or indomethacin)
    • Interosseous membrane heterotopic ossification: does not always impede rotation

    Monteggia fractures:
  • Nerve injuries: most common with Bado II/III
    • Radial/PIN
    • Median/anterior interosseous nerve (AIN)
    • Surgical exploration warranted after 3 months of observation and failure of recovery
  • Radial head instability
    • Early redislocation: consider nonanatomic reduction of the ulna or interposed tissue in the radiocapitellar joint
    • Late redislocations: may require radial head excision

    Galeazzi fractures:
  • DRUJ subluxation or dislocation: may require exploration (transposed tissue) and/or fixation with K-wire or tension band
