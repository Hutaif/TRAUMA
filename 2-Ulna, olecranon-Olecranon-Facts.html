Facts
  • Most commonly occurs in elderly patients from indirect trauma (sudden pull of the triceps and brachialis)

  • Olecranon is defined as the region of the proximal ulna proximal to the coronoid that articulates with the trochlea of the distal humerus
  • The olecranon ossification center appears around 10 and fuses at 16 years
  • Coronoid process acts as an anterior buttress and is vital to stability of the elbow
    • Sublime tubercle (medial coronoid): is the insertion of the anterior bundle of the medial collateral ligament (MCL)
    • Fractures of the coronoid involving the anteromedial facet result from varus posteromedial rotation during axial loading
      • Associated with LCL injury and instability
  • Patella cubiti is a true accessory ossicle located within the triceps tendon near its insertion in the olecranon
  • All olecranon fractures are intraarticular by definition
  • With the coronoid, the olecranon forms the greater sigmoid notch that articulates with the trochlea of the distal humerus
  • Provides stability in the anteroposterior plane
  • A transverse bare area is present in the articular cartilage midway along the arc of the sigmoid notch

  • Patients typically present with inability to extend elbow against gravity, pain, swelling and ecchymosis
  • Assess NV status and identify associated injuries
  • Ulnar nerve at high risk, particularly with high-energy trauma
  • Associated elbow fractures, dislocations or fracture-dislocations are common
    • Radial head fractures and dislocations must be identified
    • Anterior olecranon fracture-dislocations: typically involve translation of the distal humerus through the olecranon with anterior translation of the radius and ulna
      • The radial head is rarely injured
    • Posterior olecranon fracture-dislocations: typically involve distal humerus through the olecranon with posterior translation of the radial head, radial head fracture, coronoid fracture and LCL injury
